'''Paul Sorensen''' (February 16, 1926 â€“ July 17, 2008) was an [[United States|American]] [[film]], [[theater]] and [[television]] [[actor]] who appeared in hundreds of roles during his career, including ''[[The Brady Bunch]]'' and ''[[Dallas (1978 TV series)|Dallas]]''.<ref name=variety>{{cite news |first=|last=|url=http://www.variety.com/article/VR1117989328.html?categoryId=25&cs=1|title=Actor Paul Sorensen dies at 82 |work= [[Variety Magazine]] |publisher=|date=2008-07-22 |accessdate=2008-08-12}}</ref> He was frequently cast in [[Western (genre)|westerns]] or as a police officer.<ref name=variety/>

Sorenson was born in [[Kenosha, Wisconsin|Kenosha]], [[Wisconsin]].<ref name=variety/> He moved to [[Hollywood, California]], in 1945 and enrolled in the [[Pasadena Playhouse]],<ref name=variety/> from which he graduated two years later.<ref name=variety/> Later in life, he was honored by the Pasadena Playhouse with a [[lifetime achievement award]].<ref name=variety/> Sorenson served with the [[United States military]] during the [[Korean War]].<ref name=variety/> He returned to California after the war and resumed [[acting]]. A [[talent agent]] signed Sorenson after watching him perform in a theater production of ''[[Born Yesterday]]''.<ref name=variety/> He was cast in his first television role as the deputy-turned-bandit [[Billy Stiles]] in the 1954-1955 [[Television syndication|syndicated]] ''[[Stories of the Century]]'', a western [[television series|series]] starring and narrated by [[Jim Davis (actor)|Jim Davis]].<ref name=variety/>

One of Sorensen's best known characters was a recurring role as Andy Bradley, a member of an [[oil]] [[cartel]], on ''Dallas''.<ref name=variety/> He appeared in recurring television roles in ''The Brady Bunch'', ''[[Barnaby Jones]]'' and [[Fred MacMurray]]'s ''[[My Three Sons]]''.<ref name=variety/> His television career, which spanned from the 1950s to the 1980s also included work on  ''[[The Mary Tyler Moore Show]]'', ''[[My Favorite Martian]]'', ''[[The Rockford Files]]'' and ''[[The Mod Squad]]''.<ref name=variety/> Sorensen was often cast in such westerns as ''[[Jefferson Drum]]'', ''[[The Rifleman]]'', ''[[Rin Tin Tin]]'', ''[[Gunsmoke]]'', ''[[Have Gun, Will Travel]]'', ''[[The High Chaparral]]'', ''[[Cheyenne (TV series)|Cheyenne]]'', [[Cimarron City (TV series)|Cimarron City]]'', ''[[Johnny Ringo (TV series)|Johnny Ringo]], ''[[Wagon Train]]'', ''[[The Virginian (TV series)|The Virginian]]'', and ''[[The Big Valley]]''.<ref name=variety/>

Sorensen's film credits included ''[[Hang 'em High]]'' and ''[[Escape to Witch Mountain]]''.

Off screen, Sorensen and twenty-four other actors founded the [[Orchard Gables Repertory Theater]] group, which ''[[Time Magazine|Time]]'' magazine has praised as "an [[oasis]] in the heart of Hollywood."<ref name=variety/> Sorensen and his wife Jacqueline also ran the [[Original Actors Workshop]].<ref name=variety/> Sorensen retired from acting during the late 1980s and early 1990s. Sorensen, his wife, and one of their two sons, Christian, all became ordained [[Minister (Christianity)|ministers]].<ref name=variety/>

He married Jacqueline May in September 1957; she died on November 14, 2002.<ref name=variety/> Paul Sorensen died on July 17, 2008, in [[Cardiff-by-the-Sea, California|Cardiff-by-the-Sea]],  [[San Diego County, California]], at the age of eighty-two.<ref name=variety/> He was survived by his sons, a sister, and a grandson.<ref name=variety/>
