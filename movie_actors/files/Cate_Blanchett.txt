'''Catherine Élise''' "'''Cate'''" '''Blanchett'''<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.allmovie.com/artist/cate-blanchett-p215038|title=Cate Blanchett biography - AllMovie|publisher=[[AllMovie]]|accessdate=22 November 2014}}</ref> ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|b|l|ɑː|n|.|tʃ|ə|t}}; born 14 May 1969) is an Australian actress of screen and stage. She has received critical acclaim and [[List of awards and nominations received by Cate Blanchett|many accolades]], including two [[Academy Award]]s, three [[Screen Actors Guild Awards]], three [[Golden Globe Awards]] and three [[British Academy Film Awards|British Academy Awards]]. Blanchett has been awarded the [[Centenary Medal]] for Service to Australian Society by the Australian government. She was appointed [[Ordre des Arts et des Lettres|Chevalier of the Order of Arts and Letters]] by the French government in 2012. In 2014, she was presented with a [[Doctor of Letters]] by [[Macquarie University]] in recognition of her extraordinary contribution to the arts, philanthropy and the community, her third honorary degree from major Australian institutions.

Blanchett came to international attention for her role as [[Elizabeth I of England]] in [[Shekhar Kapur|Shekhar Kapur's]] 1998 film ''[[Elizabeth (film)|Elizabeth]]'', for which she won the [[BAFTA Award for Best Actress in a Leading Role|BAFTA Award for Best Actress]], the [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actress in a Motion Picture – Drama|Golden Globe Award]], and earned her first [[Academy Award for Best Actress]] nomination. Her portrayal of [[Katharine Hepburn]] in [[Martin Scorsese]]'s 2004 film ''[[The Aviator (2004 film)|The Aviator]]'' brought her critical acclaim and many accolades, including the Academy Award for [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress|Best Actress in a Supporting Role]], making her the only actor to win an Oscar for portraying another Oscar-winning actor. In 2013, she starred as Jeanette "Jasmine" Francis in [[Woody Allen]]'s ''[[Blue Jasmine]]'', for which she won numerous accolades including the Academy Award for Best Actress. She is one of only six actresses to win Academy Awards in both leading and supporting acting categories, and the only Australian to win two acting Oscars. A six-time Oscar nominee, she has also received nominations for ''[[Notes on a Scandal (film)|Notes on a Scandal]]'' (2006), ''[[Elizabeth: The Golden Age]]'' (2007) and ''[[I'm Not There]]'' (2007). Blanchett's other notable films include [[The Talented Mr. Ripley (film)|''The Talented Mr. Ripley'']] (1999), [[Peter Jackson]]'s [[The Lord of the Rings (film series)|''The Lord of the Rings'' trilogy]] (2001–03) and [[The Hobbit (film series)|''The Hobbit'' trilogy]] (2012–14), ''[[Babel (film)|Babel]]'' (2006), ''[[The Curious Case of Benjamin Button (film)|The Curious Case of Benjamin Button]]'' (2008), ''[[How to Train Your Dragon 2]]'' (2014), ''[[Cinderella (2015 film)|Cinderella]]'' (2015) and ''[[Carol (film)|Carol]]'' (2015).

Blanchett has also had an extensive career on stage and is a four-time [[Helpmann Award]] winner for [[Helpmann Award for Best Female Actor in a Play|Best Female Actor in a Play]]. Her earlier roles include the title role in ''[[Electra]]'' at the [[National Institute of Dramatic Art]] (1992), [[Ophelia]] in ''[[Hamlet]]'' at the [[Belvoir St Theatre]] in Sydney (1994), Susan in ''[[Plenty (play)|Plenty]]'' in the [[West End theatre|West End]] (1999), and the title role in ''[[Hedda Gabler]]'' with the  [[Sydney Theatre Company]] (2004). From 2008 to 2013, she and her husband [[Andrew Upton]] were co-CEOs and artistic directors of the Sydney Theatre Company; her roles included [[Blanche DuBois]] in ''[[A Streetcar Named Desire (play)|A Streetcar Named Desire]]'' in Sydney, New York at the [[Brooklyn Academy of Music]] and Washington D.C. at [[John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts|the Kennedy Center]] (2009), Yelena in ''[[Uncle Vanya]]'' in Sydney, Washington D.C. at the Kennedy Center and New York at [[Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts|the Lincoln Center]] (2011), and Claire in ''[[The Maids]]'' with [[Isabelle Huppert]] in Sydney (2013) and New York at the Lincoln Center (2014).

== Early life ==
Blanchett was born on 14 May 1969 in the Melbourne suburb of [[Ivanhoe, Victoria|Ivanhoe]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.theage.com.au/news/in-depth/strongpeoplestrong-cando-cate-the-woman-whos-everywhere/2008/03/01/1204227051340.html | work=''[[The Age (newspaper)|The Age]]''| title=Can-do Cate |accessdate=13 August 2012 | first=Peter| last=Wilmoth |date=2 March 2008}}</ref> She is the middle of three children, with an older brother, Bob, who is a computer systems engineer, and a younger sister, Genevieve, who works as a theatrical designer.<ref name=hs>{{cite web|url=http://www.heraldscotland.com/arts-ents/film/cate-blanchett-on-madness-motherhood-and-working-with-woody-allen.22155506|publisher=''[[The Herald (Glasgow)]]''|title=Cate Blanchett on madness, motherhood and working with Woody Allen|date=20 September 2013|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref> Her mother, June (née Gamble),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.playbill.com/news/article/from-hedda-to-streetcar-to-vanya-the-many-colors-of-cate-blanchett|title=From Hedda to Streetcar to Vanya: The Many Colors of Cate Blanchett|publisher=''[[Playbill]]''|date=15 June 2012|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref> was an Australian property developer and teacher, and her father, Robert DeWitt Blanchett, Jr., a [[Texas]] native, was a [[United States Navy]] [[petty officer]] who later worked as an advertising executive.<ref name="rc">{{cite journal |title=Cate Blanchett's biography|publisher=[[Elle (magazine)|Elle]]|date=December 2003|accessdate=17 October 2007}}</ref><ref name="W2010">{{cite web|url=http://www.wmagazine.com/people/celebrities/2010/06/cate_blanchett |work=[[W (magazine)]]| title=With a theater company to run and her brood of boys to raise, Cate Blanchett barely has time to be a movie star. Good thing she’s a natural|first=Danielle|last=Stein|date=June 2010|accessdate=26 May 2015}}</ref><ref name=MIL>{{cite news|title=Cate Blanchett, Theatre Boss|url=http://moreintelligentlife.com/content/arts/jo-lennan/cate-blanchett-theatre-boss?page=full|accessdate=15 September 2013|newspaper=moreintelligentlife.com}}</ref> The two met while Blanchett's father's ship broke down in Melbourne.<ref name=act/> When Blanchett was ten, her father died of a heart attack, leaving her mother to raise the family on her own.<ref name=hs/><ref name=tca>{{cite episode|title=Episode #10.3|url=http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0611211/|airdate=14 December 2003|series=Inside the Actors Studio|serieslink=Inside the Actors Studio|season=10|number=3|network=[[Bravo (US TV channel)|Bravo]]}}</ref> Blanchett's ancestry includes [[English people|English]], some [[Scottish people|Scottish]], and remote [[French people|French]].<ref name=tca/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://ethnicelebs.com/cate-blanchett|title=Cate Blanchett - Celebrity Ethnicity|publisher=EthnicCelebs|accessdate=16 March 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://uk.tv.yahoo.com/daybreak-cate-blanchett-discusses-39-monument-men-39-121900036.html|title=Daybreak: Cate Blanchett discusses 'The Monument Men'|work=Yahoo!|date=4 February 2014|accessdate=16 March 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://blogs.ancestry.com/cm/2014/02/13/cate-blanchett-a-spy-in-the-family/|title=Cate Blanchett’s Surprising Spy History|publisher=[[Ancestry.com]]|accessdate=16 March 2015}}</ref>

Blanchett has described herself as being "part extrovert, part wallflower" during childhood.<ref name=hs/> She had a penchant for dressing in masculine clothing, and went through goth and punk phases during her teenaged years, shaving her head at one point.<ref name=hs/> She attended primary school in Melbourne at Ivanhoe East Primary School; For her secondary education, she attended [[Ivanhoe Girls' Grammar School]] and then [[Methodist Ladies' College, Melbourne|Methodist Ladies' College]], where she explored her passion for the performing arts.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.crikey.com.au/2005/03/30/famous-alumni-on-lathams-hit-list/ |title=Famous alumni on Latham's hit list |publisher=''[[Crikey]]''|accessdate=15 January 2010}}</ref> She studied economics and [[fine arts]] at the [[University of Melbourne]] before leaving Australia a year later to travel overseas.<ref name=hs/><ref name=NYT>{{cite web|url=http://www.nytimes.com/movies/person/215038/Cate-Blanchett/biography|title=Cate Blanchett - Biography|publisher=''[[New York Times]]''|accessdate=3 January 2015}}</ref> After a trip in Europe she traveled to Egypt where she was asked to be an extra on an Egyptian boxing movie, ''Kaboria''.<ref name=hs/><ref name=NYT/> Upon her return to Australia she then dropped from the University of Melbourne and transferred to the [[National Institute of Dramatic Art]] (NIDA) in Sydney to pursue the acting profession.<ref name=NYT/> She graduated from NIDA in 1992.<ref name=hs/>

== Career ==

=== 1992–2000 ===
Blanchett's first major stage role was opposite [[Geoffrey Rush]] in the 1992 [[David Mamet]] play ''[[Oleanna (play)|Oleanna]]'' for the [[Sydney Theatre Company]]. That year, she was cast as [[Clytemnestra]] in a production of Sophocles’ ''[[Electra]]''. A couple of weeks after rehearsals, the actress playing the title role pulled out, and director [[Lindy Davies]] cast Blanchett in the role. Her performance as Electra became one of her most acclaimed at NIDA.<ref name=act>{{cite web|url=http://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2007/02/12/disappearing-act|title=Disappearing Act|author=Lahr, John|work=The New Yorker|date=12 February 2007|accessdate=17 February 2015}}</ref> In 1993, Blanchett was awarded the Sydney Theatre Critics' Best Newcomer Award for her performance in [[Timothy Daly (playwright)|Timothy Daly]]'s ''Kafka Dances'' and won Best Actress for her performance in Mamet's ''Oleanna'', making her the first actor to win both categories in the same year.<ref name=act/> Blanchett played the role of [[Ophelia]] in an acclaimed 1994–95 [[Company B (theatre)|Company B]] production of ''[[Hamlet]]'' directed by [[Neil Armfield]], starring Rush and [[Richard Roxburgh]], and was nominated for a [[Green Room Award]].<ref name=walk/> She appeared in the 1994 TV miniseries ''[[Heartland (Australian TV series)|Heartland]]'' opposite [[Ernie Dingo]], the 1995 miniseries ''[[Bordertown (TV miniseries)|Bordertown]] ''with [[Hugo Weaving]], and in an episode of ''[[Police Rescue]]'' entitled "The Loaded Boy".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.locatetv.com/tv/police-rescue/season-3/1805746|title=Police Rescue: Season 3 Episode 5|publisher=LocateTV|accessdate=5 March 2015}}</ref> She also appeared in the 50-minute drama short ''[[Parklands (film)|Parklands]]'' (1996), which received an Australian Film Institute nomination for Best Original Screenplay.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nytimes.com/movies/movie/136547/Parklands/overview|title=Parklands (1996)|publisher=[[The New York Times]]|accessdate=23 March 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.roninfilms.com.au/person/230/kathryn-millard.html|title=Kathryn Millard|publisher=RoninFilms.com.au|accessdate=23 March 2015}}</ref>

Blanchett made her feature film debut with a supporting role as an Australian nurse captured by the [[Imperial Japanese Army|Japanese Army]] during World War II, in [[Bruce Beresford]]'s 1997 film ''[[Paradise Road (1997 film)|Paradise Road]]'', which co-starred [[Glenn Close]] and [[Frances McDormand]].<ref name=tca /> Her first leading role, also in 1997, was as Lucinda Leplastrier in [[Gillian Armstrong]]'s romantic drama ''[[Oscar and Lucinda (film)|Oscar and Lucinda]]'', opposite [[Ralph Fiennes]].<ref name=tca /> Blanchett received wide acclaim for her performance,<ref name=NYT/> and earned her first [[Australian Film Institute]] (AFI) Award nomination as Best Leading Actress, losing to [[Deborah Mailman]] in ''[[Radiance (film)|Radiance]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.aacta.org/winners-nominees/1990-1999/1998.aspx|title=1998 Winners & Nominees|work=aacta.org|publisher=[[Australian Academy of Cinema and Television Arts]]|accessdate=1 February 2015}}</ref> She won the AFI Best Actress Award in the same year for her role as Lizzie in the 1997 romantic comedy ''[[Thank God He Met Lizzie]]'', co-starring Richard Roxburgh and [[Frances O'Connor]].<ref name=NYT/> By 1997, Blanchett had accrued significant praise and recognition in her native Australia.<ref name=NYT/>

Her first high-profile international role was as [[Elizabeth I of England]] in the critically acclaimed 1998 film ''[[Elizabeth (film)|Elizabeth]]'', directed by [[Shekhar Kapur]]. The film catapulted her to stardom and her performance garnered wide recognition, earning her the [[Golden Globe Award]] and [[British Academy of Film and Television Arts|British Academy Award]] (BAFTA), and her first [[Screen Actors Guild]] (SAG) and [[Academy Award]] nomination for Best Actress.<ref name=act/><ref name=walk/> She became the first and only actress in the history of the Academy Awards to be nominated in this category for the part.<ref name=tca />  The following year, Blanchett appeared in ''[[Bangers (film)|Bangers]]'', an Australian [[short film]] written and directed by her husband, [[Andrew Upton]], part of ''[[Stories of Lost Souls]]'', a compilation of thematically-related short stories.<ref name=Dirty/> She also appeared in the [[Mike Newell (director)|Mike Newell]] comedy ''[[Pushing Tin]]'', costarring [[Billy Bob Thornton]] and [[Angelina Jolie]], with critics singling out her performance,<ref name=NYT/> and the critically acclaimed [[Anthony Minghella]] film ''[[The Talented Mr. Ripley (film)|The Talented Mr. Ripley]]'', alongside [[Matt Damon]], [[Gwyneth Paltrow]], [[Jude Law]], and [[Philip Seymour Hoffman]]. She received her second BAFTA nomination for her performance in ''The Talented Mr. Ripley''.<ref name=tca />

=== 2000–2011 ===
Already an acclaimed actress, Blanchett received a host of new fans when she appeared in [[Peter Jackson]]'s Oscar-winning [[Blockbuster (entertainment)#Blockbuster films|blockbuster]] trilogy, ''[[The Lord of the Rings (film series)|The Lord of the Rings]]'', playing the role of [[Galadriel]] in all three films.<ref name=tca /> The trilogy holds the record as the highest grossing film trilogy of all time.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.boxofficemojo.com/siteinfo/?id=1367&p=.htm|publisher=''Box Office Mojo''|title=NEW CHART: Top Trilogies|accessdate=17 October 2007|date=21 September 2004}}</ref> In addition to ''The Lord of the Rings'', [[2001 in film|2001]] also saw Blanchett diversify her portfolio with a range of roles in the dramas ''[[Charlotte Gray (film)|Charlotte Gray]]'' and ''[[The Shipping News (film)|The Shipping News]]'' and the American crime-comedy ''[[Bandits (2001 film)|Bandits]]'', for which she earned a second Golden Globe and SAG Award nomination.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.reelga.com/2013/08/the-goods-cate-blanchetts-10-best.html |work=Reel Georgia| title=The Goods: Cate Blanchett's 10 Best Performances|accessdate=9 December 2013 |date=18 August 2013}}</ref> In 2002, Blanchett appeared, opposite [[Giovanni Ribisi]], in [[Tom Tykwer]]-directed ''[[Heaven (2002 film)|Heaven]]'', the first film in an unfinished trilogy by acclaimed writer-director [[Krzysztof Kieślowski]].<ref name=walk/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://blogs.indiewire.com/theplaylist/the-essentials-5-great-cate-blanchett-performances-20130723?page=2|title=The Essentials: 5 Great Cate Blanchett Performances|work=[[Indiewire]]|date=23 July 2013|accessdate=1 March 2015}}</ref> 2003 saw Blanchett again playing a wide range of roles; Galadriel in [[The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King|the third and final installment]] of the Lord of the Rings film trilogy (which won the [[Academy Award for Best Picture]]), the [[Ron Howard]]-directed western-thriller ''[[The Missing]]'', [[Jim Jarmusch]]'s ''[[Coffee and Cigarettes]]'' — playing two roles (both against herself) — for which she received an [[Independent Spirit Award for Best Supporting Female]] nomination, and the biographical film ''[[Veronica Guerin (film)|Veronica Guerin]]'', which earned her a Golden Globe Best Actress Drama nomination.<ref name=walk/>

In 2005, she won her first Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress for her acclaimed portrayal of [[Katharine Hepburn]] in [[Martin Scorsese]]'s ''[[The Aviator (2004 film)|The Aviator]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ign.com/articles/2013/11/14/cate-blanchetts-top-10-films|title=Cate Blanchett's Top 10 Films|publisher=''[[IGN]]''|date=13 November 2013|accessdate=3 January 2015}}</ref> This made Blanchett the first actor to garner an Academy Award for playing an Oscar-winning actor.<ref>{{cite web|title=Cate Blanchett- Biography|url=http://movies.yahoo.com/person/cate-blanchett/biography.html|publisher=[[Yahoo! Movies]]|accessdate=17 June 2013}}</ref> She lent her Oscar statue to The [[Australian Centre for the Moving Image]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.goldenglobes.com/nominee-profiles/cate-blanchett-blue-jasmine-5215|title=Cate Blanchett|author=Manelis, Michele|publisher=GoldenGlobes.com|accessdate=27 February 2015}}</ref> That year, Blanchett won the Australian Film Institute Best Actress Award for her role as Tracy Heart, a former heroin addict, in the Australian film ''[[Little Fish (film)|Little Fish]]'', co-produced by her and her husband's production company, Dirty Films.<ref name=Dirty>{{cite web|url=http://sydney.edu.au/education_social_work/about/afilliations/bios/upton.shtml|title=ANDREW UPTON|publisher=[[University of Sydney]]|accessdate=27 February 2015}}</ref> Though lesser known globally than some of her other films, ''Little Fish'' received great critical acclaim in Blanchett's native Australia and was nominated for 13 Australian Film Institute awards.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rgm.com.au/portfolios/jacquelin-perske|title=Jacquelin Perske|publisher=RGM Artists|accessdate=3 January 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.abc.net.au/tv/rake/about/directors.htm|title=Rake - About|publisher=[[ABC Online]]|accessdate=23 March 2015}}</ref>

[[File:Cate blanchett crop.jpg|thumb|left|upright=0.6|Blanchett at the [[Berlin International Film Festival]], 2007]]
In 2006, she starred opposite [[Brad Pitt]] in the multi-lingual, multi-narrative ensemble drama ''[[Babel (film)|Babel]]'', directed by [[Alejandro González Iñárritu]], which received seven Academy Award nominations, the [[Steven Soderbergh]]-directed ''[[The Good German]]'' with [[George Clooney]], and the acclaimed ''[[Notes on a Scandal (film)|Notes on a Scandal]]'' opposite [[Judi Dench|Dame Judi Dench]].<ref name=NYT/><ref name=walk/> Blanchett received a third Academy Award nomination for her performance in the latter film.<ref>{{cite news|title=Oscars 2007: full list of winners and nominees|url=http://www.theguardian.com/culture/2007/feb/26/awardsandprizes.oscars2007|date=26 February 2007|accessdate=1 April 2012|location=London|work=The Guardian}}</ref>

In 2007, Blanchett was named as one of ''[[Time (magazine)|Time]]'' magazine's 100 Most Influential People in the World and also one of the most successful actresses by ''[[Forbes]]'' magazine.<ref>{{cite news|author=Corliss, Richard|url=http://content.time.com/time/specials/2007/time100/article/0,28804,1595326_1595332_1616643,00.html|title=Cate Blanchett – The 2007 TIME 100|date=3 May 2007|accessdate=1 April 2012|work=Time}}</ref> Blanchett had a [[Cameo appearance|cameo]] as Janine, [[Forensic science|forensic scientist]] and ex-girlfriend of [[Simon Pegg]]'s character in [[Edgar Wright]]'s ''[[Hot Fuzz]]'' (2007). The cameo was uncredited and she gave her fee to charity.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://filmschoolrejects.com/features/29-things-we-learned-from-the-hot-fuzz-commentary.php|title=29 Things We Learned From the ‘Hot Fuzz’ Commentary|publisher=FilmSchoolRejects.com|date=22 August 2013|accessdate=27 February 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://movies.about.com/od/hotfuzz/a/hotfuzzew041307_2.htm|title=Inside 'Hot Fuzz' with Filmmaker Edgar Wright|author=Topel, Fred|work=[[About.com]]|accessdate=27 February 2015}}</ref>

She reprised her role as Queen Elizabeth I in the 2007 sequel ''[[Elizabeth: The Golden Age]]'', and portrayed Jude Quinn, one of six incarnations of [[Bob Dylan]] in [[Todd Haynes]]' experimental film ''[[I'm Not There]]''. She won the [[Volpi Cup]] Best Actress Award at the [[Venice Film Festival]] (accepted by fellow Australian actor and ''I'm Not There'' co-star [[Heath Ledger]]), the Independent Spirit and Golden Globe Best Supporting Actress Award for her portrayal of Jude Quinn.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://entertainment.timesonline.co.uk/tol/arts_and_entertainment/film/article2640454.ece | title = Cate Blanchett as Elizabeth I is no surprise|accessdate=14 October 2007 |location=UK |work=The Times | first = Christopher| last=Goodwin|date=14 October 2007}}</ref> At the [[80th Academy Awards]], Blanchett received two Academy Award nominations—Best Actress for ''Elizabeth: the Golden Age'' and Best Supporting Actress for ''I'm Not There''—becoming the eleventh actor to receive two acting nominations in the same year, and the first female actor to receive another nomination for the reprisal of a role.<ref name="double nod">{{Cite news|url=http://www.theaustralian.com.au/news/cates-double-oscar-nod/story-e6frg6n6-1111115377883 |title=Cate's double Oscar nod|accessdate=29 December 2014|work=[[The Australian]]|first1=Peta | last1=Hellard|date=23 January 2008}}</ref> Of her achievement that year, critic [[Roger Ebert]] said, "That Blanchett could appear in the same [[Toronto Film Festival]] playing Elizabeth and Bob Dylan, both splendidly, is a wonder of acting".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://rogerebert.suntimes.com/apps/pbcs.dll/article?AID=/20071011/REVIEWS/710110302 |title=Elizabeth: The Golden Age Movie Review (2007) |author=[[Roger Ebert]] |date=11 October 2007|accessdate=3 January 2015 |publisher=''RobetEbert.com''}}</ref>

She next appeared in [[Steven Spielberg]]'s ''[[Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull]]'', as the villainous [[KGB]] agent Col. Dr. Irina Spalko, Spielberg's favorite villain from the [[Indiana Jones (franchise)|entire series]],<ref>{{cite web|url=http://news.moviefone.com/2008/04/17/ford-spielberg-and-lucas-talk-indiana-jones/|title=Ford, Spielberg and Lucas Talk 'Indiana Jones'|publisher=[[Moviefone]]|date=17 April 2008|accessdate=27 February 2015}}</ref> and in [[David Fincher]]'s Oscar-nominated ''[[The Curious Case of Benjamin Button (film)|The Curious Case of Benjamin Button]]'', co-starring with Brad Pitt for a second time. On 5 December 2008, Blanchett was honoured with a star on the [[Hollywood Walk of Fame]] at [[List of stars on the Hollywood Walk of Fame|6712 Hollywood Boulevard]] in front of [[Grauman's Egyptian Theatre]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.theage.com.au/articles/2008/12/06/1228257368395.html |title=Blanchett gets star on Walk of Fame|accessdate=21 December 2008|date=6 December 2008|work=The Age|location=Melbourne, Australia}}</ref> Blanchett voiced the character of Granmamare for the English version of the film ''[[Ponyo]]'', released July 2008,<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.theguardian.com/film/2008/nov/27/hayaomiyazaki|title=English-language cast announced for Miyazaki's Ponyo on the Cliff|work=The Guardian|accessdate=30 November 2008|date=27 November 2008| location=London| first=Ben| last=Child}}</ref> and appeared opposite [[Russell Crowe]] in [[Ridley Scott]]'s ''[[Robin Hood (2010 film)|Robin Hood]]'' in 2010. In June 2011, she attended the premiere of her film ''[[Hanna (film)|Hanna]]'', directed by [[Joe Wright]], at the Sydney Film Festival.<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2011/06/08/cate-blanchett-sydney-film-festival_n_873254.html |work=[[The Huffington Post]] |first=Hilary| last=Moss|title=Cate Blanchett Suits Up For Sydney Film Festival (PHOTOS, POLL) |date=8 June 2011}}</ref>

Blanchett returned to the theater in 2009 with the Sydney Theatre Company production of [[Tennessee Williams]]' ''[[A Streetcar Named Desire (play)|A Streetcar Named Desire]]'', directed by [[Liv Ullmann]]. She starred as [[Blanche DuBois]] alongside [[Joel Edgerton]] as [[Stanley Kowalski]]. Ullmann and Blanchett had been meaning to collaborate on a project since Ullman's intended film adaption of ''[[A Doll's House]]'' fell by the wayside. Blanchett proposed embarking on ''Streetcar'' to Ullmann, who jumped at the opportunity after initial discussion.<ref>{{cite episode|title=The Charlie Rose Show|url=http://www.imdb.com/title/tt1564516/|series=[[Charlie Rose (TV series)|Charlie Rose]]|network=[[PBS]]|season=12|number=8|date=10 December 2009|accessdate=16 March 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite episode|url=http://www.imdb.com/title/tt1638158/?ref_=tt_ep_pr |title=Masterclass: Liv Ullmann|series=[[Masterclass (TV series)|Masterclass]]|network=[[HBO]]|season=1|number=2|date=25 April 2010|accessdate=16 March 2015}}</ref> The production traveled from Sydney to the [[Brooklyn Academy of Music]] in New York, and the [[John F. == Personal life ==
Blanchett's husband is playwright and screenwriter [[Andrew Upton]]. They met in 1996 on the set of a TV show,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.people.com/people/article/0,,20008317,00.html|title=Cate Blanchett: Getting married is insanity|date=12 January 2007|publisher=People.com|accessdate=12 February 2013}}</ref> and were married on 29 December 1997.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.celebritybrideguide.com/cate-blanchett-wedding-regrets/|title=Cate Blanchett's wedding regrets|date=10 December 2008|publisher=Celebrity Bride Guide|accessdate=12 February 2013}}</ref> Blanchett and Upton have four children: biological sons Dashiell John (b. 2001),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://celebritybabies.people.com/2009/12/04/cate-blanchett-and-dashiell-hop-to-it/|title=Cat Blanchett and Dashiell hop to it|date=4 December 2009|publisher=People.com|accessdate=12 February 2013}}</ref> Roman Robert (b. 2004),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://usatoday30.usatoday.com/life/people/2004-04-26-blanchett-baby_x.htm|title=Cate Blanchett gives birth to a son|date=26 April 2004|publisher=People.com|accessdate=12 February 2013}}</ref> Ignatius Martin (b. 2008),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://celebritybabies.people.com/2008/04/13/cate-blanchett-6/|title=Cate Blanchett welcomes third son, Ignatius Martin|date=13 April 2008|publisher=People.com|accessdate=12 February 2013}}</ref> and adopted daughter Edith Vivian Patricia,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/cate-blanchett-has-adopted-a-779802|title=Cate Blanchett Has Adopted a Baby Girl|work=The Hollywood Reporter|date=6 March 2015|accessdate=6 March 2015}}</ref> whose adoption was confirmed on 6 March 2015.<ref>{{cite web|first=Michael|last=Rothman|publisher=ABC News|title=Cate Blanchett and Husband Andrew Upton Adopt a Baby Girl|date=6 March 2015|url=http://abcnews.go.com/Entertainment/cate-blanchett-welcomes-daughter/story?id=29443419|accessdate=6 March 2015}}</ref> 

After making [[Brighton]], England their main family home for nearly 10 years, she and her husband returned to their native Australia in 2006.<ref>{{cite episode|title=Charlie Rose|series=[[Charlie Rose (TV series)|Charlie Rose]]|url=http://www.charlierose.com/watch/60244479|season=21|number=228|publisher=Charlierose.com |airdate=22 July 2013|accessdate=5 March 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mindfood.com/article/cate-blanchett-a-wicked-talent/|title=Cate Blanchett: A wicked talent|website=MiNDFOOD|first=Michele|last=Manelis|date=May 18, 2015|accessdate=May 19, 2015}}</ref> In November 2006, Blanchett stated that this was due to a desire to decide on a permanent home for her children, and to be closer to her family as well as a sense of belonging to the Australian (theatrical) community.<ref name="vogue">{{cite web|title=Head First| author=Michael Specter|date=November 2006|work=Vogue|accessdate=17 October 2007}}</ref> She and her family live in ''Bulwarra'', an 1877 sandstone mansion in the harbourside Sydney suburb of [[Hunters Hill, New South Wales|Hunters Hill]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.smh.com.au/news/people/cates-green-house/2007/07/07/1183351513267.html |title=Welcome to Cate Blanchett's dream eco-home|publisher=''[[The Sydney Morning Herald]]''|date=8 July 2007|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref> It was purchased for [[AU$]]10.2&nbsp;million in 2004 and underwent extensive renovations in 2007 to be made more [[eco-friendly]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.smh.com.au/articles/2004/12/12/1102786948082.html| title=Cate buys mansion for $10m|work=The Sydney Morning Herald|author=Hannah Edwards|date=12 December 2004|accessdate=17 October 2007}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.smh.com.au/news/people/cates-green-house/2007/07/07/1183351513267.html |title=Welcome to Cate Blanchett's dream eco-home|author=Hannah Edwards|date=8 July 2007|work=The Sydney Morning Herald|accessdate=17 October 2007}}</ref>

web|url=http://www.smh.com.au/entertainment/movies/sydney-film-festival-to-kick-off-with-hanna-premiere-20110511-1eidu.html|title=Sydney Film Festival to kick off with Hanna premiere|date=11 May 2013}}</ref> She became a spokesperson for and the face of [[SK-II]], the luxury skin care brand owned by [[Procter & Gamble]], in 2005, having used the brand years prior at the recommendation of a makeup artist friend.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2012/12/04/cate-blanchett-pictures-hair_n_2228419.html|title=Cate Blanchett On Her Teenage Goth Years, Making Worst-Dressed Lists Bazaar''|accessdate=12 February 2013}}</ref> In 2007, Blanchett became the ambassador for the [[Australian Conservation Foundation]]'s online campaign&nbsp;– trying to persuade Australians to express their concerns about climate change.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://tmagazine.blogs.nytimes.com/2011/04/15/vanishing-act/|title=Vanishing Act|date=15 April 2011|author=Daphne Merkin|publisher=New York Times|accessdate=12 February 2013}}</ref> Opening the 2008 9th World Congress of Metropolis in Sydney, Blanchett said: "The one thing that all great cities have in common is that they are all different. All cities do face similar, significant trends in the future ... most importantly global warming and climate change."<ref>{{Cite news| url = http://news.theage.com.au/national/cities-under-spotlight-at-conference-20081023-570i.html| title = Cities under spotlight at conference|work=The Age |location=Australia| agency=AAP| date = 23 October 2008| accessdate = 23 October 2008}}</ref> At the beginning of 2011, Blanchett lent her support for a [[Carbon Pollution Reduction Scheme|carbon tax]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.cleanenergyfuture.gov.au/ |title=Clean Energy Australia |date= |accessdate=2014-08-23}}</ref> She received some criticism for this, particularly from conservatives.<ref>[http://www.smh.com.au/environment/climate-change/i-want-to-be-able-to-look-my-children-in-the-face-20110530-1fd01.html/ "Blanchett support for Carbon Tax"], ''[[The Sydney Morning Herald]]''</ref> In January 2014, Blanchett took part in the Green Carpet Challenge, an initiative to raise the public profile of [[sustainable fashion]], founded by Livia Firth of Eco-Age. She wore a pair of Fairmined earrings set with responsibly sourced diamonds by the luxury Jeweller [[Chopard]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://eco-age.com/the-golden-globes-2014-with-chopard-and-cate-blanchett/|title=Cate Blanchett Wears Chopard’s Green Carpet Collection to win Best Actress at the Golden Globes|publisher=Eco-age.com|date=12 January 2015|accessdate=6 March 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Cate Blanchett Wears Chopard’s "Green Carpet Challenge" Earrings to 2014 Golden Globes|url=http://www.ecouterre.com/cate-blanchett-wears-chopards-green-carpet-challenge-jewelry-to-2014-golden-globes/|accessdate=6 March 2015|publisher=Ecouterre.com|date=13 January 2014}}</ref> Blanchett is a patron of the new [[Venice_Biennale#Australia|Australian Pavilion]] in the [[Venice Biennale]], and spoke at its opening at the Venice Giardini on 5 May 2015.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.australiacouncil.gov.au/news/media-centre/media-releases/launch-of-the-new-australian-pavilion-in-the-historic-venice-biennale-gardens/|title=LAUNCH OF THE NEW AUSTRALIAN PAVILION IN THE HISTORIC VENICE BIENNALE GARDENS|publisher=[[Australia Council for the Arts]]|date=23 April 2015|accessdate=23 April 2015}}</ref> Blanchett, who spoke at former [[Prime Minister of Australia]] [[Gough Whitlam]]'s state funeral in 2014, will speak at the [[Margaret Whitlam]] dinner and fundraiser event hosted by [[Tanya Plibersek]] [[Member_of_parliament#Australia|MP]] on 19 June 2015.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.smh.com.au/federal-politics/political-news/cate-blanchett-to-speak-at-tanya-plibersek-fundraiser-20150529-ghcpkx.html|title=Cate Blanchett to speak at Tanya Plibersek fundraiser|work=[[The Sydney Morning Herald]]|date=29 May 2015|accessdate=29 May 2015}}</ref>

In 2006, a portrait of Cate Blanchett and family painted by McLean Edwards was a finalist for the [[List of Archibald Prize winners|Archibald Prize]].<ref>{{cite web| url = http://www.thearchibaldprize.com.au/06/finalists/archibald| title=Archibald Prize 06|website=Art Gallery NSW|accessdate=26 February 2008|archiveurl=//web.archive.org/web/20071016211102/http://thearchibaldprize.com.au/06/finalists/archibald <!-- Bot retrieved archive --> |archivedate = 16 October 2007}}</ref> Another portrait of Blanchett was a finalist for the Archibald Prize in 2014.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.artgallery.nsw.gov.au/prizes/archibald/2014/29497/|title=Tim Maguire: Cate, take 1 / Cate, take 2|website=Art Gallery NSW|accessdate=31 May 2015}}</ref> In early 2009, Blanchett appeared in a series of special edition postage stamps called "Australian Legends of the Screen", featuring Australian actors acknowledged for the "outstanding contribution they have made to Australian entertainment and culture".<ref name="AustPost">{{cite web| title = Academy winners are stamped as 2009 Legends| url = http://www.auspost.com.au/BCP/0,1467,CH4661%257EMO19,00.html| publisher=[[Australia Post]]| accessdate = 5 January 2009}}</ref> She, [[Geoffrey Rush]], [[Russell Crowe]], and [[Nicole Kidman]] each appear twice in the series: once as themselves and once in character; Blanchett is depicted in character from ''[[Elizabeth: The Golden Age]]''.<ref name="AustPost" />

In 2008, Blanchett and her husband became co-CEOs and artistic directors of the [[Sydney Theatre Company]].<ref name=brw>{{Cite web| url = http://www.brw.com.au/p/leadership/cate_blanchett_on_why_being_ceo_2NSYcMtFqq3vGhz3jhnOyL | title = Cate Blanchett on why being CEO is a matter of instinct | publisher=[[BRW (magazine)|Business Review Weekly]] | date=27 June 2014| accessdate=17 November 2014}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web| url=http://dailyreview.crikey.com.au/a-class-act-how-cate-blanchett-saved-stc | title = A CLASS ACT: THE DISCREET CHARM OF CATE BLANCHETT | author=Margot Saville | date= 27 June 2014 | work=dailyreview.crikey.com.au | accessdate=17 November 2014}}</ref> The 2013 season with the Sydney Theatre Company was Blanchett's final one as co-CEO and artistic director.<ref name=brw/><ref>{{cite web|title=Cate Blanchett's next step|url=//www.youtube.com/watch?v=WO5lm1vNVQg|work=YouTube|date=6 September 2012|accessdate=22 April 2013}}</ref> Blanchett has said: "Theatre: the making of it, the consumption of it, at its best has an aspect of the ambulance chase. It's walking the precipice of an imminent disaster, the crash, the missteps, the cock-up, the collapse. That energy and secret hope in the audience has to be harnessed ... Anything live, and truly 'alive' will contain seeds of danger."<ref>{{cite web|url=http://032c.com/2013/cate-blanchett/|title=Cate Blanchett|accessdate=6 January 2014|publisher=032c}}</ref>

Blanchett has spoken about feminism and politics, telling [[Sky News]] in 2013 that she was concerned that "a wave of conservatism sweeping the globe" was threatening women's role in society.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://news.sky.com/story/1143020/cate-blanchett-equality-for-women-being-lost|title=Cate Blanchett: Equality For Women Being Lost|work=smh.com|accessdate=20 January 2014}}</ref> She has also commented on the pressures women in Hollywood face now: "Honestly, I think about my appearance less than I did ten years ago. People talk about the golden age of Hollywood because of how women were lit then. You could be [[Joan Crawford]] and [[Bette Davis]] and work well into your 50s, because you were lit and made into a goddess. Now, with everything being sort of gritty, women have this sense of their use-by date."<ref>{{cite web|url=http://femmagazine.com/cate-blanchett-feminisms-sweetheart/|title=Cate Blanchett: Feminism's Sweetheart|publisher=femmagazine.com|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref>

In 2015, [[Madame Tussauds]] unveiled a wax figure of Blanchett draped in a recreation of the [[Valentino Garavani]] dress she wore to the 2005 Academy Awards ceremony.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.madametussauds.com/hollywood/newsandevents/cate-blanchett.aspx|title=Cate Blanchett Arrives in Hollywood|publisher=[[Madame Tussauds]]|accessdate=2 April 2015}}</ref>

Blanchett's family will be relocating to the United States after her husband's term as artistic director of the Sydney Theatre Company ends in 2015. They announced it would not be a permanent move, as they would return to their native Australia later.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.smh.com.au/entertainment/theatre/andrew-upton-cate-blanchett-will-move-to-us-at-end-of-year-20150222-13lf65.html|title=Andrew Upton, Cate Blanchett will move to US at end of year|work=[[The Sydney Morning Herald]]|date=22 February 2015|accessdate=27 February 2015}}</ref>

== Awards and achievements ==
{{main|List of awards and nominations received by Cate Blanchett}}
[[File:Cate Blanchett Cannes 2014.jpg|thumb|upright|Cate Blanchett at the [[2014 Cannes Film Festival]]]]
Among her numerous accolades for her performances, Blanchett has received two [[Academy Awards]], three [[British Academy Film Awards|British Academy Awards]], four [[AACTA|Australian Academy Awards]], three [[Screen Actors Guild Awards]], three [[Golden Globe Awards]], three [[Critics Choice Awards]], two [[Film Independent Spirit Awards|Independent Spirit Awards]], four [[Helpmann Awards]], and awards from the [[Volpi Cup|Venice Film Festival]], the [[New York Film Critics Circle]], [[Los Angeles Film Critics Association]], [[National Society of Film Critics]], and the [[National Board of Review]]. Her performance as [[Katharine Hepburn]] in ''[[The Aviator (2004 film)|The Aviator]]'', made her the only actor to win an Oscar for portraying another Oscar-winning actor.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://variety.com/2015/film/news/cate-blanchett-how-to-train-your-dragon-3-oscar-speeches-1201430186/|title=Cate Blanchett on ‘How to Train Your Dragon 3′ and the Fear of Oscar Speeches|work=Variety|date=10 February 2015|accessdate=16 February 2015}}</ref> Blanchett is only the third actress, after [[Jessica Lange]] and [[Meryl Streep]], to win [[Academy Award for Best Actress|Best Actress]] after winning [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress|Best Supporting Actress]].<ref name=GD>{{cite web|url=http://www.goldderby.com/news/5056/cate-blanchett-meryl-streep-jessica-lange-film-news-13579086.html |title=Will Cate Blanchett join Meryl Streep and Jessica Lange in Oscars upgrade lounge?|publisher=Goldderby.com|accessdate=23 November 2014}}</ref> She is one of only five actors (and the only actress) in the history of the Oscars to be nominated twice for portraying the same role in two films ([[Elizabeth I of England|Elizabeth I]] in the films ''[[Elizabeth (film)|Elizabeth]]'' and ''[[Elizabeth: The Golden Age]]''), and the eleventh actor to receive two acting nominations in the same year.<ref name="double nod"/> She is also the only Australian actor to win two acting Oscars.<ref>{{cite news|title=Cate Blanchett wins best actress Oscar for Blue Jasmine|date=30 March 2014|work=[[News.com.au]]|publisher=[[News Limited]] |url=http://www.news.com.au/entertainment/awards/cate-blanchett-wins-best-actress-oscar-for-blue-jasmine/story-e6frfpli-1226843906329|accessdate=23 November 2014}}</ref>

Blanchett received the 2008 [[Santa Barbara International Film Festival]] Modern Master Award in recognition of her accomplishments in the film industry.<ref>{{cite web|title=SBIFF - Cate Blanchett, Modern Master|url=http://thearlingtontheatre.com/news/item/34-sbiff-cate-blanchett-modern-master|date=14 December 2007|publisher=[[Arlington Theater]]|accessdate=25 November 2014}}</ref> She received the Premiere Icon Award in 2006, and was honored by [[Women in Film and Television International]] with the [[Women in Film Crystal + Lucy Awards|Crystal Award]] for excellence in the entertainment industry in 2014.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1696&dat=20060921&id=WgsbAAAAIBAJ&sjid=X0gEAAAAIBAJ&pg=5404,2540050&hl=en|title=Women in Hollywood Spotlights several|newspaper=[[The Daily News (Kentucky)|The Daily News]]|page=2A|date=22 September 2006|accessdate=18 March 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.playbill.com/news/article/women-in-film-honors-cate-blanchett-kerry-washington-rose-byrne-and-more-206398|title=Women In Film Honors Cate Blanchett, Kerry Washington, Rose Byrne and More|publisher=[[Playbill]]|date=1 April 2014|accessdate=24 November 2014}}</ref> Blanchett was awarded the [[Centenary Medal]] for Service to Australian Society by the Australian government in 2007.<ref name=Macuni/> She was appointed [[Ordre des Arts et des Lettres|Chevalier of the Order of Arts and Letters]] by the [[Minister of Culture (France)|French Minister of Culture]] in 2012, in recognition of her significant contributions to the arts.<ref>{{cite web|title=Cate Blanchett was conferred the insigna of Chevalier de l'Ordre des Arts et des Lettres|url=http://www.ambafrance-au.org/Cate-Blanchett-is-confered-the|accessdate=27 February 2014|website=Embassy of France in [[Canberra]]}}</ref> In 2014, she was presented with a [[Doctor of Letters]] from [[Macquarie University]], her third [[honorary degree]] from major Australian institutions.<ref name=Macuni>{{cite web|title=Cate Blanchett receives honorary doctorate from Macquarie University|url=http://mq.edu.au/newsroom/2014/09/25/cate-blanchett-receives-honorary-doctorate-from-macquarie-university/|date=25 September 2014|publisher=Macquarie University, Sydney|accessdate=25 November 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Blanchett receives honorary doctorate|url=http://www.skynews.com.au/news/showbiz/arts/2014/09/26/blanchett-receives-honorary-doctorate.html|accessdate=30 September 2014}}</ref>

Blanchett received a Star on the [[Hollywood Walk of Fame]] on 5 December 2008. Guests included [[David Fincher]], [[Kathleen Kennedy (film producer)|Kathleen Kennedy]] and [[Steven Spielberg]]. She was inducted at 6712 Hollywood Boulevard.<ref name=walk>{{cite web|url=http://www.walkoffame.com/cate-blanchett|title=Cate Blanchett - Hollywood Walk of Fame|publisher=Walk of Fame|accessdate=28 November 2014}}</ref>
