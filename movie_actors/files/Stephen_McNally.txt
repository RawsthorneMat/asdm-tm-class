'''Stephen McNally''' (July 29, 1911 – June 4, 1994) was an [[United States|American]] [[actor]] remembered mostly for his appearances in many [[Western (genre)|westerns]] and action films. 

==Early years==
Born in [[New York City]], McNally attended [[Fordham University School of Law]]<ref name=nyt/> and was an [[Lawyer|attorney]] in the late 1930s before he pursued his passion for  acting.<ref>{{cite news|title=News and Comment Of Stage and Screen|url=http://www.newspapers.com/clip/2492502/fitchburg_sentinel/|agency=Fitchburg Sentinel|date=November 19, 1940|page=5|via = [[Newspapers.com]]|accessdate = May 26, 2015}} {{Open access}}</ref> He was a one time president of the Catholic Actors Guild.

==Career==
He started his stage career using his real name '''Horace McNally''' and began appearing uncredited in many World War II-era films.  In 1948, he changed his stage name to '''Stephen McNally''' (taking the name of his then-2-year-old son)<ref>{{cite news|last1=Carroll|first1=Harrison|title=Hollywood|url=http://www.newspapers.com/clip/2492387/the_evening_independent/|agency=The Evening Independent|date=April 23, 1948|page=4|via = [[Newspapers.com]]|accessdate = May 26, 2015}} {{Open access}}</ref> and began appearing credited as both movie villains and heroes. In 1940, he had a leading role in the stage version of ''Johnny Belinda''.<ref>{{cite news|title='Johnny Belinda' Gets New Blood|url=http://www.newspapers.com/clip/2492701/the_brooklyn_daily_eagle/|agency=The Brooklyn Daily Eagle|date=August 30, 1940|page=9|via = [[Newspapers.com]]|accessdate = May 26, 2015}} {{Open access}}</ref>

He played menacing roles in such films as ''[[Johnny Belinda (1948 movie)|Johnny Belinda]]'' (1948)<ref>{{cite news|last1=Sheaffer|first1=Lew|title=Screen|url=http://www.newspapers.com/clip/2492793/the_brooklyn_daily_eagle/|agency=Brooklyn Eagle|date=October 2, 1948|page=14|via = [[Newspapers.com]]|accessdate = May 26, 2015}} {{Open access}}</ref> and the [[James Stewart]] western ''[[Winchester '73 (1950 film)|Winchester '73]]'' (1950).  He co-starred in the [[Burt Lancaster]] film noir ''[[Criss Cross (film)|Criss Cross]]'' (1949). Other notable 1950s films included ''[[No Way Out (1950 film)|No Way Out]]'' (1950), ''[[Split Second (1953 film)|Split Second]]'' (1953), and ''[[Johnny Rocco (1958 film)|Johnny Rocco]]'' (1958).

McNally was cast in three episodes of the [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]] religion [[anthology series]] ''[[Crossroads (1955 TV series)|Crossroads]].'' He portrayed [[Monsigneur]] Harold Engle in "Ringside Padre" (1956) and [[Father Flanagan]] of the [[Boys Town (organization)|Boys Town]] orphanage in [[Nebraska]] in "Convict 1321, Age 21" (1957). In between, he was cast as [[United States Army]] [[General]] [[George S. Patton, Jr.]], in "The Patton Prayer" (also 1957).<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0573640/|title=Stephen McNally|publisher=[[Internet Movie Data Base]]|accessdate=February 15, 2013}}</ref>

McNally also co-starred on the "Ben Courtney Story" episode of ''[[Wagon Train]]'' as a former Union soldier turned Sheriff in 1958. In 1959, he portrayed Clay Thompson, a [[bounty hunter]], with [[Myron Healey]] as a sheriff, in the [[CBS]] western series, ''[[The Texan (TV series)|The Texan]]'', starring [[Rory Calhoun]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ctva.biz/US/Western/Texan.htm|title=''The Texan''|publisher=Classic Television Archive|accessdate=January 31, 2013}}</ref>

McNally was thereafter cast in the 1960 episode "Moment of Fear" of the CBS/[[Four Star Television]] anthology series, ''[[The DuPont Show with June Allyson]]'', with episode co-stars [[Edgar Bergen]] and [[Darryl Hickman]]. Thereafter, he appeared in the [[NBC]] anthology series, ''[[The Barbara Stanwyck Show]]'', and in the [[Darren McGavin]] western series, ''[[Riverboat (TV series)|Riverboat]]''. In 1961, he portrayed the part of Sky Blackstorm in the episode "Incident of the Blackstorms" on CBS's ''[[Rawhide (TV series)|Rawhide]]''.

In the 1961–62 season, McNally and [[Robert Harland]] had their own [[crime drama]] on ABC, another Four Star Production called ''[[Target: The Corruptors!]]''. The program aired on Friday in a good time slot after the popular ''[[77 Sunset Strip]]'', but it failed to gain renewal for a second season. McNally played a crusading [[newspaper]] reporter in the series, with Harland was his undercover agent. During the 1970s, McNally guest starred in television programs such as ''[[Fantasy Island]]'' and [[James Garner]]'s ''[[The Rockford Files]]'' and ''[[Police Story (TV series)|Police Story]]''.

==Death==
McNally died of a [[myocardial infarction|heart attack]] June 4, 1994,at age 82 , at his home in [[Beverly Hills, California]]. He was survived by his wife, Rita, eight children and eight grandchldren.<ref name=nyt>{{cite news|title=Stephen McNally, 82, Actor in Villain Roles|url=http://www.nytimes.com/1994/06/11/obituaries/stephen-mcnally-82-actor-in-villain-roles.html|accessdate=27 May 2015|agency=The New York Times|date=June 11, 1994}}</ref>
