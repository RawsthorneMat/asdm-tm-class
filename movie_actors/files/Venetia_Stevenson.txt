'''Venetia Stevenson''' (born 10 March 1938) is an English-American film and television actress.

==Early life==
Born in 1938 in London, England as '''Joanna Venetia Invicta Stevenson''', she is the daughter of film director [[Robert Stevenson (director)|Robert Stevenson]] and actress [[Anna Lee]]. The family moved to Hollywood within a year of her birth after her father signed a contract with film producer [[David Selznick]].<ref>Fredda Dudley "The Beautiful British", ''[[Photoplay|Photoplay/Movie Mirror]]'', May 1943</ref> When her parents divorced in 1944, she stayed with her father and new stepmother, Frances.<ref name=stepmother>"Film's Venetia Stevenson Wins Divorce", ''Los Angeles Times'', 2 April 1957, p. B1</ref> After an education in exclusive Californian private schools,<ref name=divorce>"Dream Girl Venetia's Career Is Nightmare", ''[[Los Angeles Times]]'', 10 May 1959, p. E1</ref> her theatrical debut was with her mother in ''[[Liliom]]'', a play produced by the Sombrero Theater, in [[Phoenix, Arizona]], in April 1955 and also with the husband and wife team of [[Fernando Lamas]] and [[Arlene Dahl]].<ref>"Lamas, Dahl Praised For 'Liliom'", ''Los Angeles Times'', 2 April 1955, p. 15</ref>

A one-time Miss Los Angeles Press Club,<ref>"Mayor Will Crown Miss Press Club", ''Los Angeles Times'', 16 July 1961, p. G10</ref> Stevenson was placed on contract by [[RKO Pictures]] in November 1956.<ref name=movie>"Grossinger News-Notes", ''New York Times'', 11 November 1956, p.    175</ref> [[Hedda Hopper]] named Stevenson on her list of top movie newcomers in January 1957, alongside [[Jayne Mansfield]]. Hopper said of Stevenson, then 18, she is "the most purely beautiful of all the new crop of stars."<ref>"Hedda Hopper Names Top Movie Discoveries During 1956", ''Los Angeles Times'', 6 January 1957, p. E1</ref>

==Film and television actress==
On 13 March 1957, Stevenson was cast in [[CBS]]'s ''[[Playhouse 90]]'' adaptation of ''[[Charley's Aunt]]'', alongside [[Tom Tryon]], [[Jackie Coogan]], and [[Jeanette MacDonald]].<ref>"Terhune Stories Will Be TV Series", ''New York Times'', 13 March 1957, p. 63</ref> On 12 November 1957, Stevenson appeared as Kathy Larsen in the episode "Trail's End" of the [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]]/[[Warner Brothers]] [[Western (genre)|western]] series, ''[[Sugarfoot]]''. In the story line Kathy is a former childhood sweetheart of Tom Brewster, played by series star [[Will Hutchins]] though Stevenson is eight years younger than Hutchins. She is managing a dance hall to his consternation. [[Chris Alcaide]] plays the corrupt Clay Horton, who forces Kathy to marry him so that she cannot testify in court in regard to Horton's numerous crimes. [[Barbara Stuart]] is cast as Muriel, Kathy's business partner. [[Gordon Jones (actor)|Gordon Jones]] plays Sugarfoot's lively friend, Wasco Wolters, who has a romantic interest in Muriel. This episode reveals that Tom Brewster spent his childhood in [[Vermont]] before coming to the [[Oklahoma Territory]]. Stevenson subsequently appeared in two other ''Sugarfoot'' episodes, including "Brink of Fear" (1958) with fellow co-stars [[Jerry Paris]], [[Harry Antrim]], [[Allen Case]], and [[Don Gordon (actor)|Don Gordon]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0712930/|title=''Sugarfoot'': "Trail's End", November 12, 1957|publisher=[[Internet Movie Data Base]]|accessdate=25 August 2012}}</ref>

Stevenson played Peggy McTavish in the 1958 film, ''[[Darby's Rangers (1958 film)|Darby's Rangers]]'', a Warner Brothers release in which she was paired off with [[Peter Brown (actor)|Peter Brown]]. She is one of the women who is pursued by actors cast as members of an American unit of the same name during [[World War II]]. The movie was directed by [[William Wellman]].<ref>"Screen: Amorous G.I.'s", ''New York Times'', 13 February 1958, p. 23</ref>

Stevenson's publicity machine continued to promote her. She was reported enjoying riding horses as an activity<ref name=Tab>''Louella Parsons'', ''Lincoln Evening Journal'', 26 July 1959, p. 18</ref> and playing table tennis.<ref name=movie/> In November 1957, she won $300 in prizes at a horse show and participated at the National Horse Show at the [[Cow Palace]] in San Francisco.<ref>"Venetia's Only in Love With Riding Horses", ''Los Angeles Times'', 3 November 1957, p. F1</ref> Around this time she became the face on Sweetheart Stout cans and bottles;<ref>http://www.cannyscot.com/SweetheartStout.htm</ref> the brand marked the 50th anniversary of using her image in 2008.

She appeared in 1959 in the western drama ''[[Day of the Outlaw]]'' (1959), starring [[Robert Ryan]] and [[Tina Louise]].<ref>"Day of the Outlaw Snowbound Western", 10 September 1959, p.C15</ref> Stevenson also had a primary role in the film version of the [[Studs Lonigan]] trilogy by [[James T. Farrell]], brought to the screen in December 1960.<ref>"Columbia To Back Italian Producer", ''New York Times'', 14 December 1960, p. 51</ref> Among the other motion pictures in which she appears are ''[[Island of Lost Women]]'' (1959), ''[[Jet Over the Atlantic]]'' (1959), ''[[The Big Night (1960 film)|The Big Night]]'' (1960), ''[[Seven Ways from Sundown]]'' (1960), ''[[The City of the Dead (film)|The City of the Dead]]'' aka ''Horror Hotel'' (1960), and ''The Sergeant Was a Lady'' (1961).

Stevenson appeared on television, in episodes of ''[[Cheyenne (TV series)|Cheyenne]]'' (1957), ''[[Colt .45 (TV series)|Colt .45]]'' (1958), ''[[77 Sunset Strip]]'' (1958), ''[[The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet]]'' (1958), ''[[Lawman (TV series)|Lawman]]'' (1958), ''[[The Millionaire (TV series)|The Millionaire]]'' (1959), ''The Third Man'' (1959), and ''[[Alfred Hitchcock Presents]]'' (1960) alongside [[Burt Reynolds]] and [[Harry Dean Stanton]].

==Personal life==
Stevenson married [[MGM]] actor-dancer, [[Russ Tamblyn]], on [[Valentine's Day]], 1956, shortly after her half-brother, actor [[Jeffrey Byron]], was born to her mother. She was 17 when she and Tamblyn had their wedding in the [[Wayfarers Chapel]] in [[Palos Verdes]].<ref>"Actor-Model To Wed on Valentine's Day", ''Long Beach Independent-Press-Telegram'', 12 February 1956, p. 12</ref> Stevenson and Tamblyn divorced in April 1957,<ref name=stepmother/> but the two remained friends.<ref>"Hedda Hopper Hollywood", ''The Lima News'', 4 July 1959, p. 19</ref> A widely-reproduced photo shows Stevenson calmly walking down a Los Angeles street, seemingly unaware that Tamblyn is doing a spectacular backward aerial handspring a few inches away from her.

Stevenson remarried, to [[Don Everly]], in 1962 and retired from acting and modelling. The couple had two daughters, Stacy and Erin Everly, both model/actresses, and a son, [[Edan Everly]], a musician. She divorced Don Everly in 1970 and has not remarried. Erin, the ex-wife of rocker [[Axl Rose]], was the inspiration for several Guns N' Roses songs including [[Sweet Child o' Mine]], where she also appeared in the video.
