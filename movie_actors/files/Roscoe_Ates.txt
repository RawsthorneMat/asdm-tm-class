'''Roscoe Ates''' (January 20, 1895 – February 12, 1962) was an American vaudeville performer, actor of stage and screen, comedian and musician who primarily featured in [[Western (genre)|western]] films and television.

==Early years==

Ates was born in the rural hamlet of Grange, northwest of [[Hattiesburg, Mississippi|Hattiesburg]], Mississippi. Grange is no longer included on road maps. Ates spent much of his childhood overcoming a severe [[speech impediment]].<ref name=allmov>{{cite web|url=http://www.allmovie.com/cg/avg.dll?p=avg&sql=2:2680|title=''Roscoe Ates": Biography by Hal Erickson|publisher=allmovie.com|accessdate=March 1, 2009}}</ref> He entered the entertainment medium as a concert violinist but found economic opportunities greater as a [[vaudeville]] comedian. He revived his long-gone stutter for humorous effect. Besides his early films, Ates starred in his own short subject series with [[RKO]] and [[Vitaphone]].<ref name=allmov/>

His first film role was at the age of thirty-four in 1929 as a ship's cook in ''South Sea Rose''. The next year he was cast as "Old Stuff" in the film ''[[Billy the Kid (1930 film)|Billy the Kid]]'' with [[Johnny Mack Brown]] (1904–1974) as [[Billy the Kid]] and [[Wallace Beery]] (1885–1949) as Deputy Sheriff [[Pat Garrett]].

==Film career==
In 1931, Ates appeared in a total of fourteen films, some roles uncredited. Here is a partial listing of his films:

*''[[Check and Double Check]]'' (1930) as Brother Arthur, film based on the [[Amos 'n' Andy]] Radio program
*''[[Cimarron (1931 film)|Cimarron]]'' (1931) as Jesse Rickey, based on the [[Edna Ferber]] novel about [[Oklahoma]]
*''[[The Champ (1931 film)|The Champ]]'' (1931), with Wallace Beery and [[Jackie Cooper]]
*''Renegades of the West'' (1932), as Dr. Henry Fawcett
*''[[Freaks]]'' (1932)
*''[[Lucky Devils (1933 film)|Lucky Devils]]'' (1933)
*''[[Alice in Wonderland (1933 film)|Alice in Wonderland]]'' (1933) as Fish
* ''[[Fair Exchange (film)|Fair Exchange]]'' (1936), as Elmer Goodge
*''God's Country and the Woman'' (1937) as Gander Hopkins
*''[[The Great Adventures of Wild Bill Hickok]]'' (1938) as Oscar "Snake-Eyes" Smith. [[Wild Bill Elliott|Bill Elliott]] played Hickok.
*''[[Gone with the Wind]]'' (1939) as a convalescing Confederate soldier. While scratching his back on a tent pole, he utters the line "These animules is driving me crazy!"
*''[[Three Texas Steers]]'' (1939), a [[John Wayne]] film, features Ates as Sheriff Brown
*''Cowboy from Sundown'' (1940), a [[Tex Ritter]] film, with Ates as Deputy Gloomy Day
*''[[Captain Caution]]'' (1940)
*''Bad Men from [[Missouri]]'' (1941) as Lafe.<ref name=imdb>{{cite web|url=http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0040374/|title=Roscoe Ates|publisher=[[Internet Movie Data Base|IMDB]]|accessdate=March 1, 2009}}</ref>

==Soapy Jones==
From 1946 to 1948, Ates appeared as the western character Soapy Jones in fifteen films, including ''Colorado Serenade'', ''Driftin' River'' (with [[Shirley Patterson]]), ''Stars Over Texas'', and ''Tumbleweed Trail'' (all 1946), ''West to Glory'', ''Shadow Valley'', and ''Wild Country'' (all 1947), and ''Check Your Guns'', ''Black Hills'', ''Tornado Range'', ''The Westward Trail'', and ''The Tioga Kid'' (all 1948).<ref name=imdb/> His Soapy Jones character is the sidekick to the "Singing Cowboy" portrayed by native [[Texas|Texan]], [[Eddie Dean (singer)|Eddie Dean]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.b-westerns.com/pals8.htm|title=Saddle Pals and Cowboy Sidekicks|publisher=B-Westerns.com|accessdate=March 1, 2009}}</ref> Thereafter, [[George "Gabby" Hayes]] employed archival footage from many Soapy Jones films in his 1950s children's television series, ''[[The Gabby Hayes Show]]''.<ref name=imdb/>

In 1950, Ates was cast in his first television role as Deputy Roscoe in the short-lived [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]] series ''[[The Marshal of Gunsight Pass]]'', which was broadcast live from a primitive studio lot in Los Angeles, California. Eddie Dean also appeared in this program, as did [[Jan Sterling]] in the role of Roscoe's much younger girlfriend.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.tv.com/the-marshal-of-gunsight-pass/show/8883/summary.html|title=''The Marshal of Gunsight Pass'': Summary|publisher=TV.com|accessdate=March 1, 2009}}</ref>

==Television career==
Ates soon appeared on television in multiple roles. He was cast as Henry Wilson in the episode "The Census Taker" of the [[Television syndication|syndicated]] western series ''[[The Cisco Kid (TV series)|The Cisco Kid]]'', starring [[Duncan Renaldo]] and [[Leo Carrillo]]. He appeared that same year in the [[Gale Storm]] sitcom, ''[[My Little Margie]]'' and on the detective series ''[[Boston Blackie]]''. He appeared on [[Gail Davis]]'s ''[[Annie Oakley (TV series)|Annie Oakley]]'' series as Curly Dawes, the telegraph operator,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.retroland.com/pages/retropedia/tv/item/396/|title=Annie Oakley|publisher=retroland.com|accessdate=January 11, 2010}} {{Dead link|date=November 2010|bot=H3llBot}}</ref> in "Showdown at Diablo" (1956) and as Walsh in "Annie and the Miser" (1957). Ates played The Ranger in the 1957 episode "Sorrowful Joe Returns" of ABC's ''[[The Adventures of Rin Tin Tin]]''.<ref name=imdb/>

In 1958, the 63-year-old Ates was cast as "Old Timer" in the episode "The Sacramento Story" of [[NBC]]'s ''[[Wagon Train]]'' starring [[Ward Bond]]. That same year he was Edwin Winkler in the episode "Force of Habit" of [[Lee Marvin]]'s NBC [[crime drama]], ''[[M Squad]]''. In 1959, Ates appeared as Juniper Dunlap in "The Painted Beauty" episode of [[John Payne (actor)|John Payne]]'s NBC western, ''[[The Restless Gun]]'', as Dusty Peabody in "The Man from Solitary" of [[Rod Cameron (actor)|Rod Cameron]]'s syndicated western crime drama ''[[State Trooper (TV series)|State Trooper]]'', and as Harrison in "A Well of Gold" on [[Tom Nolan (actor)|Tom Nolan]]'s NBC ''[[Buckskin (TV series)|Buckskin]]'' series. He had a nameless role as a barfly in the 1958 episode of "Maverick" called "Gun-Shy", a spoof of the series Gunsmoke.  In 1960, he was cast as Fenton in the episode "Hot Ice Cream" of [[Charles Bronson]]'s ABC series ''[[Man with a Camera (TV series)|Man with a Camera]]'', as Lou Nugget in "The Fabulous Fiddle" of [[Scott Brady]]'s syndicated ''[[Shotgun Slade]]'', and as Deputy Boak in "The Missing Queen" of [[Andrew Duggan]]'s ABC crime drama ''[[Bourbon Street Beat]]'', set in [[New Orleans]].<ref name=imdb/>

==Recurring role on ''Lawman''==
From 1959–1960, Ates appeared once as "Old Timer" and in seven episodes as Ike Jenkins in the [[John Russell (actor)|John Russell]] and [[Peter Brown (actor)|Peter Brown]] ABC western series ''[[Lawman (TV series)|Lawman]]'', set in [[Laramie, Wyoming]]. The episodes are entitled "The Visitor", "The Gang", "The Ring", "The Friend", "The Exchange", "The Breakup", "The Stranger", and "Man on the Mountain".<ref name=imdb/>

During this same time frame as he appeared on ''Lawman'', Ates guest starred as Renton in two episodes entitled "Long Odds" of [[Dale Robertson]]'s ''[[Tales of Wells Fargo]]'' and four times on ABC's ''[[Maverick (TV series)|Maverick]]'' in episodes entitled "Gun-Shy", "Two Beggars on Horseback", "Two Tickets to Ten Strike" (with [[Connie Stevens]] and [[Adam West]], with Ates cast as Joe the Barber), and "Hadley's Hunters". In 1960, he appeared as a [[bartender]] in the episode "The Rape of Red Sky" of NBC's ''[[The Outlaws (1960 TV series)|The Outlaws]]''. He appeared in [[Will Hutchins]]'s ABC western, ''[[Sugarfoot]]'', in the 1960 episode "The Man from Eudora".<ref name=imdb/>

==Later roles==

From 1958–60, Ates appeared five times on [[Columbia Broadcasting System|CBS]]'s ''[[Alfred Hitchcock Presents]]'' mystery series. In the 1958 episode "And the Desert Shall Blossom," Ates and [[William Demarest]], later of ''[[My Three Sons]]'', appear as two old timers Tom and Ben, respectively, who are living in the Nevada desert. The local sheriff, played by [[Ben Johnson (actor)|Ben Johnson]], appears with an eviction notice, but he agrees to let the men stay on their property if they can make a dead rosebush bloom within the next month.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://google.com/search?q=cache:C9CYxthdog8J:www.fandango.com/roscoeates/filmography/p2680+Roscoe+Ates&hl=en&ct=clnk&cd=19&gl=us|title=Roscoe Ates Filmography|publisher=Fandango.com|accessdate=March 1, 2009}}</ref> Ates also appeared in the Hitchcock episode "The Jokester" in the role of Pop Henderson.<ref name=imdb/>

In 1960, Ates appeared as a guest in the presentation of the life story of honorary [[Hollywood]] mayor [[Johnny Grant (radio personality)|Johnny Grant]] on NBC's ''[[This Is Your Life]]'' biography series with host [[Ralph Edwards]].<ref name=imdb/>

Ates's last credited roles were in 1961 as a drunk in [[Robert Stack]]'s ABC series ''[[The Untouchables (1959 TV series)|The Untouchables]]'' and as sheriffs in ''[[The Red Skelton Show]]'' in an episode entitled "Candid Clem" and in "Three for One" of NBC's ''[[Whispering Smith (TV series)|Whispering Smith]]'' starring [[Audie Murphy]]. His final screen appearance in [[Jerry Lewis]]'s 1961 film ''[[The Errand Boy]]'' was uncredited.<ref name=imdb/>

==Family and death==

Ates was married three times. After his divorce from the former Clara Callahan, he married Leonore Belle Jumps in 1949. She died in 1955. In 1960, he married Beatrice Heisser who survived him.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nndb.com/people/262/000173740/|title=Roscoe Ates|publisher=NNDB.com|accessdate=March 1, 2009}}</ref>

Ates died of lung cancer at the age of sixty-seven in Hollywood.<ref name=imdb/> He is [[entombment|entombed]] at [[Forest Lawn Memorial Park, Glendale|Forest Lawn Memorial Park]] in [[Glendale, California]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.findagrave.com/cgi-bin/fg.cgi?page=gr&GRid=6675|title=Roscoe Ates burial site|publisher=Findagrave.com|accessdate=March 1, 2009}}</ref>
