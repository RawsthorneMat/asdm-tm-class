'''Donald John "Don" DeFore''' (August 25, 1913 – December 22, 1993) was an American film, radio, and television actor. DeFore is best known as Erskin "Thorny" Thornberry, the Nelson family's neighbor on the long running sitcom ''[[The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet]]'' and as George "Mr. B." Baxter on the 1960s sitcom ''[[Hazel (TV series)|Hazel]]''.

==Early life==
DeFore was one of seven children born in [[Cedar Rapids, Iowa]] to Joseph Ervin, a railroad engineer who worked at the [[Chicago and North Western Transportation Company]] and was also a local politician, and Albina Sylvia DeFore (née Nezerka).<ref>{{cite book|title=Motion Picture, Volumes 69-70|date=1945|publisher=Macfadden-Bartell|page=26}}</ref> DeFore's mother, who occasionally directed plays at their local church, was of [[Czechoslovakia]]n descent.<ref name=readingthomas>{{cite news|last=Thomas|first=Bob|title=Actor Defore Relates Czechoslovakian Tale|newspaper=Reading Eagle|date=August 22, 1969|page=12}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title=Don DeFore Shatters Myth|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1356&dat=19640429&id=eYFPAAAAIBAJ&sjid=BwUEAAAAIBAJ&pg=6060,6026719|newspaper=Ocala Star-Banner|date=April 29, 1964|page=28}}</ref>   After graduating from [[Washington High School (Cedar Rapids, Iowa)|Washington High School]] in Cedar Rapids, DeFore attended [[Iowa University]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Don DeFore, Iowan In Hollywood, Still Has Corn-Fed Look|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=2506&dat=19461006&id=5c08AAAAIBAJ&sjid=BS4MAAAAIBAJ&pg=6607,916898|newspaper=The News and Courier|date=October 6, 1946|page=6-D}}</ref>  He initially studied law while also playing basketball, track, and baseball before becoming interested in acting. Since acting was not a major study at the university, he left and enrolled at the [[Pasadena Community Playhouse]], where he won a scholarship and stayed for three years.<ref>{{cite news|title=Actor Don De Fore Devoted Family Man|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1946&dat=19621128&id=zsAtAAAAIBAJ&sjid=Cp4FAAAAIBAJ&pg=7158,5752732|newspaper=The Montreal Gazette|date=November 28, 1962|page=11}}</ref>

During this time he and four fellow students wrote a play called ''Where Do We Go From Here''. It was presented in a little theater in Hollywood with Don in the cast. As a young man, DeFore toured the country in stock companies for several years before making his Broadway debut in 1938, when Oscar Hammerstein II offered to take it to Broadway and DeFore and five of the original cast members went along. The show ran for four weeks, and Don was soon recognized as a member of legitimate theater. He remained in New York and won a key role in ''The Male Animal'', which ran for almost a year on Broadway and eight months on the road.

==Career==
In Hollywood, DeFore's first screen appearance was in a bit part in 1936's ''Reunion''. By the early 1940s and billed as Don DeForest, he was last cast in speaking roles in multiple film appearances including: ''[[Right to the Heart]]'' (1942), ''[[The Male Animal]]'' (1942), ''[[The Human Comedy (film)|The Human Comedy]]'' (uncredited, 1943), ''[[A Guy Named Joe]]'' (1943), ''[[Thirty Seconds over Tokyo]]'' (1944), ''[[The Affairs of Susan]]'' (1945), ''[[You Came Along]]'' (1945), ''[[Without Reservations]]'' (1946), ''[[It Happened on 5th Avenue]]'' (1947), ''[[Ramrod (film)|Ramrod]]'' (1947), ''[[Romance on the High Seas]]''  (1948), ''[[My Friend Irma (film)|My Friend Irma]]'' (1949), ''[[Too Late for Tears]]'' (1949), ''[[Dark City (1950 film)|Dark City]]'' (1950), ''[[Southside 1-1000]]'' (1950), ''[[She's Working Her Way Through College]]'' (1952), ''[[The Guy Who Came Back]]'' (1951), ''[[A Girl in Every Port (1952 film)|A Girl in Every Port]]'' (1952), ''[[Jumping Jacks]]'' (1952), ''[[Battle Hymn (film)|Battle Hymn]]'' (1957), ''[[A Time to Love and a Time to Die]]'' (1958), and ''[[The Facts of Life (film)|The Facts of Life]]'' (1960).

In 1946, exhibitors voted him the fourth-most promising "star of tomorrow".<ref>{{cite news |url=http://nla.gov.au/nla.news-article17994035 |title=The Stars of To-morrow. |newspaper=[[The Sydney Morning Herald]] |date=10 September 1946 |accessdate=24 April 2012 |page=11 Supplement: The Sydney Morning Herald Magazine |publisher=National Library of Australia}}</ref> DeFore also worked in radio. He appeared on such radio programs as ''[[Old Gold Comedy Theater]]'' and [[Lux Radio Theater]].

DeFore is best known for his work in television. Beginning in 1952, DeFore had a recurring role as the Nelsons' friendly neighbor, "Thorny", on the [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]] sitcom ''[[The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet]]''.<ref name=sarasota>{{cite news|title=Actor Don DeFore dies; he was Ozzie's neighbor|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1755&dat=19931224&id=YDIcAAAAIBAJ&sjid=-XsEAAAAIBAJ&pg=2251,3774077|newspaper=Sarasota Herald-Tribune|date=December 24, 1993|page=3A}}</ref> In 1955, DeFore was nominated for a [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Comedy Series|Best Supporting Actor in a Regular Series]] [[Primetime Emmy Award]] for his work on ''The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet''. In time though, the role of Thorny was superseded by [[Lyle Talbot]] as Joe Randolph, and [[Mary Jane Croft]] as his wife, Clara.

From 1954 to 1955, he served as president of the [[National Academy of Television Arts & Sciences]]. He was instrumental in arranging for the [[Emmy Awards]] to be broadcast on national television for the first time on March 7, 1955.<ref name=sarasota/> He also served on the board of the [[Screen Actors Guild]].<ref>{{cite news|title='Ozzie and Harriet,' 'Hazel' actor Don Defore dies at 80|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1920&dat=19931223&id=y40fAAAAIBAJ&sjid=i9MEAAAAIBAJ&pg=4227,4130851|newspaper=The Volusian|date=December 24, 1993|page=3C}}</ref>

From 1961 to 1965, DeFore was a co-star of the television series ''[[Hazel (TV series)|Hazel]]'' as "Mr. B." (George Baxter), employer of the spirited, domineering housekeeper Hazel Burke, played by [[Shirley Booth]] and based on a character in ''[[The Saturday Evening Post]]''.<ref name=sarasota/> Interestingly, DeFore was not the original actor to portray George Baxter.  In the pilot episode, the role was played by character actor Edward Andrews.  DeFore took over the role when the series was green-lighted.  The series ran on [[prime time]] until 1966 when it was canceled by [[NBC]]. DeFore and his co-star [[Whitney Blake]] were written out of the series when [[CBS]] picked up the series for its final season.<ref>{{cite news|last=Pack|first=Harvey|title='Hazel's' Former Lucky Returning as Sports Reporter|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=861&dat=19681117&id=9QVaAAAAIBAJ&sjid=SUsNAAAAIBAJ&pg=2887,2486755|newspaper=The Victoria Advocate|date=November 17, 1968|page=8}}</ref>

===Writing===
In 1965, DeFore and his daughter Penny wrote ''With All My Love'', a book detailing Penny's experiences working in a Korean orphanage. DeFore later released his memoirs, ''Hollywood DeFore and After''.<ref name=victoria>{{cite news|title=Jovial actor Don DeFore dies at 80|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=861&dat=19931224&id=2ChSAAAAIBAJ&sjid=7jUNAAAAIBAJ&pg=3973,1311126|newspaper=The Victoria Advocate|date=December 24, 1993|page=14A}}</ref>

==Personal life==

===Marriage and children===
[[File:Don DeFore with daughter Penny 1967.JPG|thumb|200px|Appearing with daughter, Penny, in the play, ''Generation'' in 1967.]]

DeFore married singer Marion Holmes (born Marion Holm, January 21, 1918, Chicago) on February 14, 1942. [[Judy Garland]] served as Holmes' [[matron of honor]].<ref name=barnes/> Holmes performed with the [[Henry Busse]] Orchestra from 1935 to 1939, and later with Art Kassel and his "Castles in the Air" from 1939 until their marriage.<ref>{{cite book|title=The Big Band Almanac (Revised Edition, first paperback printing March 1989)|last=Walker|first=Leo|year=1978|publisher=Da Capo Press, a subsidiary of Plenum Publishing Corporation|location=New York|isbn=0-306-80345-3|page=225}}</ref> They had five children: Penny, David, Dawn, Ron, and Autumn. They remained married until DeFore's death in 1993.<ref name=barnes>{{cite web|last=Barnes|first=Mike|title=Marion Holmes DeFore, who toured with big bands and recorded "I'm a Little Teapot", was 93.|url=http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/vocalist-wife-actor-don-defore-266516|publisher=hollywoodreporter.com|date=November 27, 2011}}</ref>

===Politics and other activities===
DeFore and his family were longtime residents of the [[Mandeville Canyon]] section of [[Brentwood, Los Angeles, California|Brentwood]] and attended the Village Church of Westwood Lutheran.<ref>{{cite news|title=Movie Players Aid Fund Drive Kickoff|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1350&dat=19510804&id=5mVRAAAAIBAJ&sjid=VAAEAAAAIBAJ&pg=3492,1185708|newspaper=Toledo Blade|date=August 4, 1951|page=5}}</ref>  DeFore served as Brentwood's honorary Mayor and also served as a member of the advisory committee for the [[California Department of Rehabilitation]].<ref name=oliver>{{cite web|last=Oliver|first=Myrna|title=Actor Don DeFore Dies; Played Mr. B on 'Hazel'|url=http://articles.latimes.com/1993-12-24/local/me-5025_1_don-defore|publisher=latimes|date=December 24, 1993}}</ref> DeFore was also a 33rd degree [[Freemasonry|Freemason]]<ref name=oliver/>

From 1957 to 1962, DeFore and his family operated the Silver Banjo Barbecue, a restaurant located in [[Frontierland]] of [[Disneyland Park (Anaheim)|Disneyland Park]] in [[Anaheim, California]].<ref name=barnes/> In July 1969, DeFore served as the American delegate at the [[Moscow International Film Festival]].<ref name=readingthomas/>

A long time [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]], DeFore was a delegate at the [[1980 Republican National Convention]]. His friend, former actor and 40th [[President of the United States]] [[Ronald Reagan]], appointed him to the Presidential Advisory Council to the [[Peace Corps]].<ref name=victoria/>

==Death==
On December 22, 1993, DeFore died of [[cardiac arrest]] at [[Saint John's Health Center]] in [[Santa Monica, California]].<ref name=victoria/> He is interred at the [[Westwood Village Memorial Park Cemetery]] in Los Angeles.<ref>{{cite book|last=Ellenberger|first=Allan R.|title=Celebrities in Los Angeles Cemeteries: A Directory|year=2001|publisher=McFarland & Company Incorporated Pub|isbn=0-786-40983-5|page=214}}</ref>

For his contribution to the television industry, Don DeFore has a star on the [[Hollywood Walk of Fame]] located at 6804 Hollywood Blvd.<ref>{{cite web|title=Hollywood Star Walk: Don DeFore|url=http://projects.latimes.com/hollywood/star-walk/don-de-fore/|publisher=latimes}}</ref>
