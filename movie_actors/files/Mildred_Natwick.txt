'''Mildred Natwick''' (June 19, 1905 – October 25, 1994) was an American stage, film and television actress. In 1967, she earned an [[Academy Award]] nomination for her [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress|supporting role]] in ''[[Barefoot in the Park (film)|Barefoot in the Park]]''. She was nominated for two [[Tony Awards]] in 1957 and 1976 and won a [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie|Primetime Emmy Award]] for her work in the [[miniseries]] ''[[The Snoop Sisters]]'', opposite [[Helen Hayes]].<ref name="milwaukee">{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=1499&dat=19941028&id=GaMaAAAAIBAJ&sjid=Hy0EAAAAIBAJ&pg=4111,3602975|title=Natwick never lost her love for the stage|date=October 28, 1994|work=The Milwaukee Journal|page=D4|accessdate=February 17, 2015|location=Milwaukee, Wisconsin}}</ref>

==Early life==
Natwick was born in [[Baltimore]], [[Maryland]], the daughter of Joseph and Mildred Marion Natwick (''née'' Dawes).<ref name="flint">{{cite web|url=http://www.nytimes.com/1994/10/26/obituaries/mildred-natwick-89-actress-who-excelled-at-eccentricity.html|title=Mildred Natwick, 89, Actress Who Excelled at Eccentricity|last=Flint|first=Peter B.|date=October 26, 1994|publisher=nytimes.com|accessdate=February 17, 2015}}</ref> Her grandfather, Ole Natwick, was one of the earliest Norwegian immigrants to the United States, arriving in Wisconsin in 1847.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Jones|first1=George O.|last2=McVean|first2=Norman S.|title=History of Wood County, Wisconsin|volume=1|year=1923|publisher=Brookhaven Press|page=362}}</ref> Her first cousin was animator and cartoonist [[Grim Natwick|Myron "Grim" Natwick]].<ref>{{cite book|last=Meuel|first=David|title=Women in the Films of John Ford|year=2014|publisher=McFarland|isbn=0-786-47789-X|page=83}}</ref>

She attended the [[Bryn Mawr School]] in Baltimore and later graduated from [[Bennett College (New York)|Bennett College]].<ref name="milwaukee"/> 

==Career==
Natwick began performing on the stage at age 21 with "The Vagabonds", a nonprofessional theatre group in Baltimore. She soon joined the [[University Players]] on [[Cape Cod]]. Natwick made her Broadway debut in 1932 playing Mrs. Noble in Frank McGrath’s play ''Carry Nation'', about the famous [[temperance movement|temperance]] crusader [[Carrie Nation]]. Throughout the 1930s she starred in a number of plays, frequently collaborating with friend and actor-director-playwright [[Joshua Logan]].<ref name="masterworks">{{cite web|url=http://www.masterworksbroadway.com/artist/mildred-natwick|title=Mildred Natwick|publisher=masterworksbroadway.com}}</ref> On Broadway, she played "Prossy" in [[Katharine Cornell]]'s production of ''[[Candida (play)|Candida]]''. She made her film debut in [[John Ford]]'s ''[[The Long Voyage Home]]'' as a Cockney prostitute, and portrayed the landlady in ''[[The Enchanted Cottage (1945 film)|The Enchanted Cottage]]'' (1945).

Natwick is remembered for small but memorable roles in several [[John Ford]] film classics, including  ''[[3 Godfathers]]'' (1948), ''[[She Wore a Yellow Ribbon]]'' (1949), and ''[[The Quiet Man]]'' (1952). She played Miss Ivy Gravely, in [[Alfred Hitchcock]]'s ''[[The Trouble with Harry]]'' (1955), and a sorceress in ''[[The Court Jester]]'' (1956).

[[File:Mildred Natwick in The Trouble With Harry trailer.jpg|thumb|Natwick in the film ''[[The Trouble with Harry]]'' in 1955]]
   
She continued to appear onstage, and made regular guest appearances in television series. She was twice nominated for [[Tony Awards]]: in 1957 for ''The Waltz of the Toreadors'', and in 1972 for the musical ''70 Girls 70''. She returned to film in ''[[Barefoot in the Park (film)|Barefoot in the Park]]'' (1967) as the mother of the character played by [[Jane Fonda]]. The role earned Natwick her only Academy Award nomination for [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress|Best Supporting actress]].<ref name="masterworks"/> One of Natwick's memorable roles was in ''[[The House Without a Christmas Tree]]'' (1972), which starred [[Jason Robards]] and [[Lisa Lucas]]. The program's success spawned three sequels: ''The Thanksgiving Treasure'', ''The Easter Promise'', and ''Addie and The King of Hearts''.

In 1971, Natwick co-starred with [[Helen Hayes]] in the ''[[ABC Movie of the Week]]'', ''[[Do Not Fold, Spindle, or Mutilate]]'', in which their characters worked together as amateur sleuths. The success of that telefilm resulted in a 1973-74 series, also called ''[[The Snoop Sisters]]'', which was part of ''[[The NBC Wednesday Mystery Movie]]''. For her performance, Natwick won the [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie]].<ref name="flint"/> In 1981, Natwick joined Hayes as the first members of the Board of Advisors to the [[Riverside Shakespeare Company]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Helen Hayes (1900-1993)|date=October 10, 2013|work=The Daily Bulletin|publisher=[[Maryland School for the Deaf]]|page=1|location=Frederick, Maryland}}</ref> Both attended and supported several fund raisers for that [[off-Broadway]] theatre company.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nytimes.com/1981/12/04/style/the-evening-hours.html|title=The Evening Hours|last=Nemy|first=Enid|date=December 4, 1981|publisher=nytimes.com|accessdate=February 17, 2015}}</ref>

She guest-starred on such television series as ''[[McMillan & Wife]]'', ''[[Family (1976 TV series)|Family]]'', ''[[Alice (TV series)|Alice]]'', ''[[The Love Boat]]'', ''[[Hawaii Five-O]]'', ''[[The Bob Newhart Show]]'', and ''[[Murder She Wrote]]''. She made her final film appearance at the age of 83 in the 1988 historical drama ''[[Dangerous Liaisons]]''.<ref name="nissen">{{cite book|last=Nissen|first=Axel |title=Actresses of a Certain Character: Forty Familiar Hollywood Faces from the Thirties to the Fifties|year=2007|publisher=McFarland|isbn=0-786-42746-9|page=132}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Natwick, who never married or had children, lived in a duplex on [[Park Avenue]] in [[Manhattan]] for the majority of her life. She was a devout [[Christian Scientist]].<ref>{{harv|Nissen|2007|p.131}}</ref>  

==Death==
On October 25, 1994, Natwick died of [[cancer]] at her home in Manhattan at the age of 89.<ref name="flint"/><ref name="nissen"/> She is interred at [[Lorraine Park Cemetery]] in Baltimore.<ref name="nissen"/>
