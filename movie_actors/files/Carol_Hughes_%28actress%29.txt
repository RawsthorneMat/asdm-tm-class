'''Carol Hughes''' (January 17, 1910 – August 8, 1995) was an American actress. She is best known for her leading roles opposite [[Gene Autry]] and [[Roy Rogers]], and for her role as Dale Arden in ''[[Flash Gordon Conquers the Universe]]'' (1940).

==Biography==
Hughes was born '''Catherine Mabel Hukill''' on January 17, 1910 in Chicago, Illinois to Charles, an upholsterer, and Mable Hukill (née Stift).<ref name="b-westerns">{{cite web|title=Carol Hughes |publisher=B-Westerns |url=http://www.b-westerns.com/ladies61.htm |accessdate=April 20, 2014}}</ref> Both of her parents were born in Chicago; her mother's grandparents were from Germany.<ref name="b-westerns"/> She was raised in a rented house at 2122 Pearl Court in Chicago along with a cousin, Pearl Hukill.<ref name="b-westerns"/> As a teenager, she was drawn to acting and participated in school plays.

At the age of fourteen, she began acting and dancing in short musical comedies with an [[Oshkosh, Wisconsin|Oshkosh]] stock company.<ref name="milwaukee-sentinel">{{cite news|last=Herzog |first=Buck |title=In and About Film Studios |newspaper=Milwaukee Sentinel |date=October 29, 1936 |url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=VkNQAAAAIBAJ&sjid=HQ0EAAAAIBAJ&pg=4692,5691974&dq=carol-hughes&hl=en |accessdate=April 20, 2014}}</ref> The following year she appeared as Katie Conway in the Conway Sisters team, having learned to sing and play piano.<ref name="milwaukee-sentinel"/> In the late 1920s, she teamed up with [[Frank Faylen]] to form the comedy dancing and singing team of "Faylen and Hughes" in which she played the "dumb girl" Kitty Hughes.<ref name="milwaukee-sentinel"/> In 1928, the eighteen-year-old Hughes married Frank Faylen.<ref name="st-petersburg">{{cite news|last=Keavy |first=Hubbard |title=Carol Hughes' Secret Is Out; She's Married |newspaper=St. Petersburg Times |date=January 30, 1937 |url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=IjpPAAAAIBAJ&sjid=a04DAAAAIBAJ&pg=6940,4488017&dq=carol-hughes&hl=en |accessdate=April 20, 2014}}</ref> By the early 1930s, they were performing together in stock and vaudeville shows throughout Illinois and Wisconsin.<ref name="milwaukee-sentinel"/>

In 1935, Hughes and Faylen moved to Hollywood to attempt movie careers.<ref name="st-petersburg"/> Hughes began her film career playing [[bit part]]s in ''[[George White's 1935 Scandals]]'' (1935) and ''[[Ceiling Zero]]'' (1936). After being signed on as a contract player by [[Warner Bros.]] in 1936, her first name was changed to Carol<ref name="milwaukee-sentinel"/> and she began to appear in credited speaking roles, mostly in [[B movie]]s. By 1937, she was appearing in leading roles in films such as ''Meet the Boy Friend'', ''[[Marry the Girl (1937 film)|Marry the Girl]]'', ''[[Renfrew of the Royal Mounted (1937 film)|Renfrew of the Royal Mounted]]'', and ''The Westland Case''.

[[File:Carol Hughes 1940.jpg|thumb|Hughes as Dale Arden in ''Flash Gordon Conquers the Universe'', 1940]]
In 1938, Hughes transitioned to [[Western (genre)|Western]] films appearing opposite [[Gene Autry]] in ''[[Gold Mine in the Sky]]'' (1938), ''[[Man from Music Mountain]]'' (1938), and ''[[Under Fiesta Stars]]'' (1941), and opposite [[Roy Rogers]] in ''[[Under Western Stars]]'' (1938).<ref name="b-westerns"/> In 1940 she landed one of her best known roles as Dale Arden in ''[[Flash Gordon Conquers the Universe]]'' (1940).<ref name="b-westerns"/>

Throughout the early 1940s, Hughes appeared in leading roles in ''Top Sergeant Mulligan'' (1941), ''[[The Miracle Kid]]'' (1941), ''My Son, the Hero'' (1943), ''She's for Me'' (1943), and ''The Beautiful Cheat'' (1945). As the decade progressed, however, she appeared increasingly in minor roles in films such as ''[[The Bachelor and the Bobby-Soxer]]'' (1947), ''[[Every Girl Should Be Married]]'' (1948), and ''[[Mighty Joe Young (1949 film)|Mighty Joe Young]]'' (1949). In 1947, Hughes returned to the stage to perform in ''Trouble for Rent''.<ref name="windsor">{{cite news|last=Oakley |first=Annie |title=The Theatre and Its People |newspaper=The Windsor Daily Star |date=April 12, 1947 |url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=QBk_AAAAIBAJ&sjid=YU8MAAAAIBAJ&pg=3649,4742626&dq=carol-hughes&hl=en |accessdate=April 20, 2014}}</ref>

Hughes made brief returns to Western films in leading roles in ''[[Home in Oklahoma]]'' (1946) with Roy Rogers, and ''[[Stagecoach Kid]]'' (1949) with [[Tim Holt]]—the latter film featured her last major screen role. Minor roles followed in ''[[D.O.A. (1950 film)|D.O.A.]]'' (1950) and ''[[Scaramouche (1952 film)|Scaramouche]]'' (1952). In the last years of her film career, Hughes appeared mainly in [[RKO Pictures|RKO]] film shorts and one television series episode.<ref name="b-westerns"/>

Hughes' husband [[Frank Faylen]] continued working as a character actor in films and television through the 1970s. He appeared in supporting roles in numerous films, including ''[[The Pride of the Yankees]]'' (1942), ''[[The Lost Weekend (film)|The Lost Weekend]]'' (1945), Frank Capra's ''[[It's a Wonderful Life]]'' (1946) in the role of Ernie the cabdriver, ''[[Road to Rio]]'' (1948), ''[[Gunfight at the O.K. Corral (film)|Gunfight at the OK Corral]]'' (1965), and ''[[Funny Girl (film)|Funny Girl]]'' (1968).<ref name="find-a-grave-feylen">{{cite web|title=Frank Faylen |publisher=Find a Grave |url=http://www.findagrave.com/cgi-bin/fg.cgi?page=gr&GRid=7083516 |accessdate=April 20, 2014}}</ref> He is best known today for playing the father Herbert T. Gillis in the television series ''[[The Many Loves of Dobie Gillis]]'' (1959-1963). Hughes and Faylen raised two daughters, Catherine (or Kay) and Carol, both of whom became actresses. Their daughter Kay was the first wife of [[Regis Philbin]]. Their daughter Carol appeared in the television series ''[[Leave It to Beaver]]'' (1962–1963) and ''[[The Bing Crosby Show]]'' (1964–1965). Hughes and Faylen remained married for fifty-seven years until his death in 1985.

Carol Hughes died on August 8, 1995 in [[Burbank, California]] at the age of 85. She is buried next to her husband in [[San Fernando Mission Cemetery]] in Mission Hills, Los Angeles, California.<ref name="find-a-grave-hughes">{{cite web|title=Carol Hughes |publisher=Find a Grave |url=http://www.findagrave.com/cgi-bin/fg.cgi?page=gr&GRid=10044980 |accessdate=April 20, 2014}}</ref>
