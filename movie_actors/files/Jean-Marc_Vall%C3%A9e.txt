'''Jean-Marc Vallée''' (born March 9, 1963) is a [[Canada|Canadian]] [[film director]], [[screenwriter]], and [[film editor]] from [[Québec]]. After studying film at the [[Université du Québec à Montréal]], Vallée went on to make a number of acclaimed short films, including ''Stéréotypes'' (1991), ''Les Fleurs magiques'' (1995), and ''Les Mots magiques'' (1998).

His debut feature, ''[[Black List (1995 film)|Black List]]'' (1995), was nominated for nine [[Genie Awards]], including nods for Vallée's direction and editing. His fourth feature film, ''[[C.R.A.Z.Y.]]'' (2005), received further critical acclaim and was a financial success.  Vallée's followup, ''[[The Young Victoria]]'' (2009), garnered strong reviews and received three [[Academy Award]] nominations, while his sixth film, ''[[Café de Flore (film)|Café de Flore]]'' (2011), was the most nominated film at the [[32nd Genie Awards]]. Vallée's next film, ''[[Dallas Buyers Club]]'', was released in 2013 to critical acclaim and earned him an [[Academy Award]] nomination in the category of [[Academy Award for Best Film Editing|Best Film Editing]]. He next directed ''[[Wild (film)|Wild]]'' (2014).

==Life and career==

===Early work===
Jean-Marc Vallée was born on March 9, 1963 in [[Montreal]], [[Quebec]].<ref name="allocine"/> He studied [[filmmaking]] at the [[Collège Ahuntsic]] and the [[Université du Québec à Montréal]].<ref name="allocine"/>

In the 1990s, Vallée produced a number of short films that aroused considerable [[film criticism|critical]] interest.<ref name="tiff"/> In 1991, ''Stereotypes'', a [[fantastique]] comedy inspired by some American classic films, received numerous prizes at several events, including Best Promising Director for Vallée at the [[Rendez-vous du cinéma québécois]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://gpa-films.com/fr/fiche_ster.html|title=Stéréotypes|publisher=GPA Films|accessdate=December 9, 2012|language=French}}</ref> Vallée later adopted a more personal and autobiographical tone with ''Les Fleurs magiques'' (1995) and ''Les Mots magiques'' (1998), awarded respectively Best Short Film at the [[16th Genie Awards]] and the [[Jutra Award|1st Jutra Awards]], in which the director explored the relationship between father and son.<ref name="tiff">{{cite web|url=http://tiff.net/CANADIANFILMENCYCLOPEDIA/content/bios/jean-marc-vallee|title=Jean-Marc Vallée|first=Liz|last=Czach|work=Canadian Film Encyclopedia|publisher=[[Toronto International Film Festival]]|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref>

Vallée made his feature-length debut in 1995 with ''[[Black List (1995 film)|Liste noire]]'' (''Black List''), which became the highest-grossing film in Quebec that year and received nine [[Genie Award]] notimations, including [[Genie Award for Best Motion Picture|Best Motion Picture]] and [[Genie Award for Best Achievement in Direction|Best Achievement in Direction]].<ref name="telequebec"/> In the wake of this success, Vallée moved to [[Los Angeles]] where he directed ''[[Los Locos]]'' (1998), a Western film written by and starring [[Mario Van Peebles]], and ''Loser Love'' (1999).<ref name="telequebec">{{cite web|url=http://cinemaquebecois.telequebec.tv/#/Artisans/88/Clips/1026/Default.aspx|title=Jean-Marc Vallée|publisher=[[Télé-Québec]]|accessdate=December 9, 2012|language=French}}</ref> After these two low-budget productions, he directed two episodes of the television series ''[[The Secret Adventures of Jules Verne]]'' (2000).

===''C.R.A.Z.Y.''===
During the mid-1990s, Vallée was preparing ''[[C.R.A.Z.Y.]]'' from a screenplay inspired by his own youth and the one of his co-writer, François Boulay. Vallée wanted to shoot the film in the United States, but his friend [[Michel Côté (actor)|Michel Côté]], who also starred in ''Black List'', convinced him to shoot in Quebec.<ref name="allocine">{{cite web|url=http://www.allocine.fr/personne/fichepersonne-17171/biographie/|title=Jean-Marc Vallée : Sa biographie|work=[[AlloCiné]]|publisher=Tiger Global|first=Alexandre|last=Jourdain|accessdate=December 9, 2012|language=French}}</ref> After ten years in production, ''[[C.R.A.Z.Y.]]'' was finally released in 2005 and became one of the most successful films in Quebec history, both financially and critically.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.variety.com/article/VR1117940032/|title=Quebec kudos just 'C.R.A.Z.Y.' for pic|work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|publisher=[[Penske Business Media]]|first=Brendan|last=Kelly|date=March 20, 2006|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref> It tells the story of Zachary Beaulieu, a young man dealing with [[homophobia]] and [[heterosexism]] while growing up with four brothers and a [[conservative]] father in 1960s and 1970s Quebec. The role of Zachary Beaulieu was portrayed by [[Marc-André Grondin]], while Michel Côté and [[Danielle Proulx]] starred as Zachary's parents. ''[[C.R.A.Z.Y.]]'' had its world premiere at the [[2005 Toronto International Film Festival]] and was awarded Best Canadian Feature Film.<ref name="canada.com"/> It received unanimous praise from film critics, with the film aggregator website, [[Rotten Tomatoes]], giving the film a 100% "Certified Fresh" rating, based on reviews from 17 critics.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/crazy/|title=C.R.A.Z.Y. (2005)|publisher=[[Rotten Tomatoes]]|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref> It received several accolades, including eleven [[Genie Awards]] and thirteen [[Jutra Awards]].<ref name="canada.com">{{cite news|url=http://www.canada.com/montrealgazette/story.html?id=d0a4367a-b29a-4b8b-b658-c474d982c6c2&k=75110|title=Jutra judges wild about C.R.A.Z.Y.|work=''Canada.com''|publisher=[[Postmedia News]]|date=March 20, 2006|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref> ''C.R.A.Z.Y.'' was also selected as Canada's official submission for the 2005 [[Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.indiewire.com/article/dispatch_from_l.a._hoping_for_oscar_attention_a_trio_of_foreign_language_ti|title=Hoping for Oscar Attention, A Trio of Foreign Language Titles Win Over Audiences|publisher=[[IndieWire]]|date=November 9, 2005|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref>

===''The Young Victoria''===
After the success of ''C.R.A.Z.Y.'', [[Graham King]] and [[Martin Scorsese]] hired Jean-Marc Vallée to direct the period drama ''[[The Young Victoria]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.telegraph.co.uk/culture/film/4344459/The-Young-Victoria-we-were-amused.html|title=The Young Victoria: we were amused|work=[[The Daily Telegraph]]|first=Chloe|last=Fox|date=February 4, 2009|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref> Written by [[Julian Fellowes]], the film is based on the early life and reign of [[Queen Victoria]], and her marriage to [[Albert, Prince Consort|Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha]]. The film stars [[Emily Blunt]], [[Rupert Friend]], [[Paul Bettany]], [[Miranda Richardson]] and [[Jim Broadbent]] among a large ensemble cast. Critical reception was generally positive and the film was nominated for three [[82nd Academy Awards|Academy Awards]], winning the 2009 [[Academy Award for Best Costume Design]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://latimesblogs.latimes.com/alltherage/2010/03/academy-award-for-costume-design-goes-to-sandy-powell-for-young-victoria.html|work=[[Los Angeles Times]]|title='Young Victoria' earns Sandy Powell a third Oscar for costume design|first=Adam|last=Tschorn|date=March 7, 2010|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref>

===''Café de Flore''===
In 2011, Vallée wrote and directed ''[[Café de Flore (film)|Café de Flore]]'', a love story between a man and woman, and between a mother and her son. The film starred [[Vanessa Paradis]], [[Kevin Parent]], [[Hélène Florent]], and [[Evelyne Brochu]]. It was acclaimed by film critics and garnered thirteen nominations for the [[32nd Genie Awards]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.toronto.com/article/710366?bn=1|title=‘Café de Flore’, ‘A Dangerous Method’ lead Genie Awards race|first=Linda|last=Barnard|work=[[Toronto Star]]|date=January 17, 2012|accessdate=December 9, 2012}}</ref>

===''Dallas Buyers Club''===
Vallée's next film, ''[[Dallas Buyers Club]]'', starred [[Matthew McConaughey]], [[Jared Leto]] and [[Jennifer Garner]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/jared-leto-returning-acting-dallas-386911|title=Jared Leto Returning to Acting with 'Dallas Buyer's Club'|work=[[The Hollywood Reporter]]|last=Kit|first=Borys|date=November 6, 2012|accessdate=November 27, 2012}}</ref> The film is based on the true-life tale of [[Ron Woodroof]], a Texas electrician diagnosed with AIDS and given 30 days to live, who began smuggling [[alternative medicine]] into the United States to help himself and other AIDS patients. The film was released in 2013 to critical acclaim, earning Matthew McConaughey the [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actor – Motion Picture Drama|Golden Globe Award for Best Actor]] and Jared Leto a [[Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actor – Motion Picture|Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actor]]. The film was nominated for six [[Academy Awards]], including [[Academy Award for Best Picture|Best Picture]] and [[Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay|Best Original Screenplay]], and conquered the awards for [[Academy Award for Best Actor|Best Actor]] for McConaughey, and [[Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor|Best Supporting Actor]] for Leto, repeating the Golden Globes.

===''Wild''===
Vallée's film ''[[Wild (film)|Wild]]'', starring [[Reese Witherspoon]], premiered on August 29, 2014 at the [[Telluride Film Festival]], and was also featured at the [[Toronto Film Festival]] on September 8 and the [[San Diego Film Festival]] on September 24.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://variety.com/2014/film/news/reese-witherspoon-wild-san-diego-film-festival-1201294101/|title=Reese Witherspoon’s ‘Wild’ to Open San Diego Film Festival (EXCLUSIVE)|last=McNary|first=Dave|work=Variety|date=August 29, 2014|accessdate=August 29, 2014}}</ref> It was released in North America on December 5, 2014.<ref>{{cite web|last=Sperling|first=Nicole|title=Reese Witherspoon-starrer 'Wild' gets a release date|url=http://insidemovies.ew.com/2014/05/12/reese-witherspoon-wild-release-date|accessdate=May 13, 2014|work=Entertainment Weekly|date=May 12, 2014}}</ref>
