'''Isaac Stanford Jolley, Sr.''', known as '''I. Stanford Jolley''' (October 24, 1900 &ndash; December 7, 1978), was a '''prolific''' [[United States|American]] [[character actor]] of [[film]] and [[television]], primarily in [[Western (genre)|western]] roles as [[cowboy]]s, law-enforcement officers, or [[villain]]s. Recognized by his slight build, narrow face, and pencil-thin [[moustache]], Jolley appeared some five hundred times on the large or small screen.<ref name=amctv>{{cite web|url=http://movies.amctv.com/person/36012/I-Stanford-Jolley/details|title=Hans J. Wolstein, "I. Stanford Jolley"|publisher=movies.amctv.com|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref>

==Film roles==

Born in [[Morristown, New Jersey|Morristown]], [[New Jersey]], Jolley toured as a child with his father's traveling [[circus]] and worked in [[vaudeville]]. He first performed on [[Broadway (theatre)|Broadway]] in 1924 opposite [[Charles Trowbridge]] in ''Sweet Seventeen''. He also worked in [[radio]] until he performed his first uncredited part in the 1935 [[Bette Davis]] film, ''[[Front Page Woman]]''. He appeared in twenty-five films for [[Republic Pictures]] between 1936 and 1954, but he was never under contract to the studio. According to his wife, he never earned more than $100 on any of his multiple film appearances.<ref name=amctv/>

In 1939, he played an uncredited part as a hotel clerk in ''[[Mr. Wong in Chinatown]]''. Appearing in scores of films, mostly westerns, Jolley was cast in 1940 as Molotoff in ''[[Chasing Trouble]]'', with other performers in the comedy/espionage film including western actors [[Milburn Stone]] and [[Tris Coffin|Tristram Coffin]]. In 1942, he was cast as Gil Harkness in the western ''[[Outlaws of Boulder Pass]]''. In 1944, he was cast as Saladin in the swashbuckling "western" film set in the [[Middle East]], ''[[The Desert Hawk]]'', and as Bart Kern in the [[Tex Ritter]] film, ''[[Gangsters of the Frontier]]''.<ref name=imdb>{{cite web|url=http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0427157/|title=I. Stanford Jolley|publisher=[[Internet Movie Data Base]]|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref> In 1945, Jolley was cast as Marshal Mullins in ''[[Springtime in Texas]]'', a 55-minute film about a crime boss, Pete Grant, played by [[Rex Lease]], who controls the [[West Texas]] town of [[Pecos, Texas|Pecos]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0163278/|title=''Springtime in Texas''|publisher=imdb.com|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref>

In 1946, Jolley portrayed Dr. Blackton in ''[[The Crimson Ghost]]'' and also did the voice of the undefined title character.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.b-westerns.com/memory4.htm|title=Memories|publisher=b-westerns.com|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref> That same year, he portrayed [[Sheriff]] Bill Armstrong in ''Silver Range'' and James Beeton in the western musical, ''[[Swing, Cowboy, Swing]]''. In 1948, Jolley was cast as the loan shark Rance Carson in ''[[Tex Granger|Tex Granger, Midnight Rider of the Plains]]'', with [[Robert Kellard]] in the title role. In 1949, Jolley appeared as Professor Bryant in ''[[King of the Rocket Men]]'', again with Tristram Coffin. That same year, he was cast as Mark Simmons in ''[[Trouble at Melody Mesa]]'', starring [[Brad King (actor)|Brad King]] as a [[marshal]].  He also appeared as Toad Tyler in 1949s [[Rimfire (film)|Rimfire]]. In 1950, Jolley was cast as J.B. "Dude" Dawson in the low-budgeted Republic Pictures [[film serial]], ''[[Desperadoes of the West]]''. Also in 1950 he appeared as "Snake Willens" in the [[Audie Murphy]] western [[Sierra (film)|Sierra]]. In 1951, he was cast as Sam Fleming in ''Oklahoma Justice'', with [[Johnny Mack Brown]], with whom he had also appeared in ''Silver Range'', and as Zorol in the [[Columbia Pictures]] serial, ''[[Captain Video: Master of the Stratosphere]]'', starring [[Judd Holdren]] as [[Captain Video and His Video Rangers|Captain Video]]. In 1953, he appeared as Ted in the [[Audie Murphy]] and [[Lee Van Cleef]] film ''[[Tumbleweed (film)|Tumbleweed]]'', set on a [[wagon train]] that encounters problems with [[Native Americans in the United States|Indians]]. That same year, Jolley appeared as Rocky in the western film, ''[[Son of Belle Starr]]'', a drama about Starr's son, "The Kid" or Ed Reed, played by [[Keith Larsen]], who attempts to lead an upright life despite his family background. In 1954, he played the stationmaster in [[Vermont]] in the [[Bing Crosby]]/[[Danny Kaye]] [[Christmas]] classic ''[[White Christmas (film)|White Christmas]]''. In 1956, Jolley appeared as Henry Longtree in the short film ''[[I Killed Wild Bill Hickok]]''.<ref name=imdb/>

==Television roles==

From 1950-1953, Jolley first appeared on television with six castings in different role in the  [[television series|series]], ''[[The Lone Ranger (TV series)|The Lone Ranger]]'' with [[Clayton Moore]]. He appeared twice in 1953 in the [[Broadcast syndication|syndicated]] western series, ''[[The Range Rider]]''. He made two appearances as Parker in ''[[Tales of the Texas Rangers]]'', with series stars [[Willard Parker]] and [[Harry Lauter]]. Jolley guest starred as the henchman Walt, along with Clayton Moore and [[Darryl Hickman]], in the 1954 episode "Annie Gets Her Man" of the syndicated [[Gail Davis]] and [[Brad Johnson (television actor)|Brad Johnson]] western, ''[[Annie Oakley (TV series)|Annie Oakley]]''. He appeared as Sheriff Bascom in the 1954 episode "[[Black Bart (Stories of the Century)|Black Bart]]" of the syndicated [[Jim Davis (actor)|Jim Davis]] series, ''[[Stories of the Century]]''.<ref name=imdb/>

In 1958, Jolley appeared on [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]]'s ''[[Walt Disney anthology television series|Walt Disney Presents]]'' in the role of Sheriff Adams in the episode "Law and Order, Incorporated", with [[Robert Loggia]] as [[Elfego Baca]]. His then 32-year-old son, [[Stan Jolley]], was the art director of the segment.<ref name=isjolley>{{cite web|url=http://www.reelzchannel.com/person/150250/i-stanford-jolley|title=I. Stanford "Stan" Jolley|publisher=reelzchannel.com|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref> Others in the episode were former [[child actor]] [[Skip Homeier]] and [[Raymond Bailey]], later the banker [[Milburn Drysdale]] of [[CBS]]'s ''[[The Beverly Hillbillies]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ctva.biz/US/Western/Disney_NineLivesOfElfegoBaca.htm|title=''The Nine Lives of Elfego Baca''|publisher=Classic Television Archives|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref>

Jolley soon appeared multiple times on a wide range of other western series, including, ''[[The Adventures of Wild Bill Hickok]]'' (three times), ''[[The Cisco Kid (TV series)|The Cisco Kid]]'' (ten),  ''[[Tales of the Texas Rangers]]'' (twice), ''[[Sergeant Preston of the Yukon]]'' (twice), ''[[The Roy Rogers Show]]'' (three), ''[[The Gene Autry Show]]'' (four), ''[[Sky King]]'' (four), ''[[Death Valley Days]]'' (five), ''[[26 Men]]'' (five appearances, again with Tristram Coffin, the series star), ''[[Wanted: Dead or Alive (TV series)|Wanted Dead or Alive]]'' (two), ''[[Bronco (TV series)|Bronco]]'' (twice), ''[[Tales of Wells Fargo]]'' (twice), ''[[The Life and Legend of Wyatt Earp]]'' (six), ''[[Maverick (TV series)|Maverick]]'' (six), ''[[Lawman (TV series)|Lawman]]'' (six), ''[[Cheyenne (1955 TV series)|Cheyenne]]'' (seven), ''[[Rawhide (TV series)|Rawhide]]'' (five), ''[[Wagon Train]]'' (ten), ''[[The Virginian (TV series)|The Virginian]]'' (two), ''[[Daniel Boone (TV series)|Daniel Boone]]'' (two), ''[[Laredo (TV series)|Laredo]]'' (two), ''[[The Big Valley]]'' (three), ''[[Bonanza]]'' (eight), and ''[[Gunsmoke]]'' (nine). In 1957, he appeared once on the [[Will Hutchins]] western, ''[[Sugarfoot]]'', as the Indian character, The Nighthawk, in the episode entitled, "Reluctant Hero". In 1958, he was cast in the episode "The Horse Nobody Wanted" of the NBC children's western series, ''[[Fury (TV series)|Fury]]'', starring [[Peter Graves]] and [[Bobby Diamond]].<ref name=imdb/> In 1959, he was cast as Beriah Jackson in the episode "Contest at Gold Bottom" of the ABC/[[Warner Brothers]] western series, ''[[The Alaskans]]''.

In 1960, Jolley guest starred as the Indian named Singing Arrow in the series finale, "The Search," of the syndicated western, ''[[Pony Express (TV series)|Pony Express]]'', with [[Grant Sullivan (actor)|Grant Sullivan]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ctva.biz/US/Western/PonyExpress.htm|title=''Pony Express''|publisher=Classic Television Archives|accessdate=January 30, 2013}}</ref> In 1962, he was cast as The Stranger in the episode "Quarantine" of the NBC western series, ''[[The Tall Man (TV series)|The Tall Man]]'', starring [[Barry Sullivan (actor)|Barry Sullivan]], and [[Clu Gulager]].<ref name=imdb/>

In 1965, Jolley appeared as Enos Scoggins in "The Greatest Coward on Earth" of the [[Chuck Connors]] series, ''[[Branded (TV series)|Branded]]''. He had also appeared with Connors on ABC's ''[[The Rifleman]]'' in one of the last episodes of the series in 1963 in the role of Joe Fogner in "Hostages to Fortune" (1963). He appeared four times in 1956 in archival footage on the children's western ''[[The Gabby Hayes Show]]''.<ref name=imdb/>

Jolley's last western roles were in 1976: as (1) a farmer in ABC's ''[[The Macahans]]'', the pilot of [[James Arness]]'s second western series, ''[[How the West Was Won (TV series)|How the West Was Won]]'',<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.reelzchannel.com/person/150250/i-stanford-jolley|title=I. Stanford Jolley|publisher=reelzchannel.com|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref> and as (2) a drunkard in the short-lived [[Tim Matheson]] and [[Kurt Russell]] series ''[[The Quest (1976 TV series)|The Quest]]''.<ref name=imdb/>

Jolley's non-western appearances included ''[[The Adventures of Superman (TV series)|The Adventures of Superman]]'' (twice), ''[[Perry Mason (TV series)|Perry Mason]]'' (twice), ''[[The Untouchables (1959 TV series)|The Untouchables]]'' as Pete Laffey in "The Man in the Cooler", ''[[Profiles in Courage (TV series)|Profiles in Courage]]'' in the episode "[[Andrew Johnson]]" (with [[Walter Matthau]] in the title role), ''[[Man with a Camera (TV series)|Man with a Camera]]'' as Dr. Ben Todd in "The Killer", ''[[Mr. and Mrs. North]]'' as Harry in "The Ungrateful Killer", and in [[Johnny Weismuller]]'s ''[[Jungle Jim (TV series)|Jungle Jim]]'' as Bremer in "Voodoo Drums".<ref name=imdb/>

==Personal life and legacy==

Jolley and his wife, Emily Mae or "Peggy" Jolley (1901–2003),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ssdi.rootsweb.ancestry.com/cgi-bin/ssdi.cgi|title=Social Security Death Index|publisher=ssdi.rootsweb.ancestry.com|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref> had two children, the art director Stanford Jolley, Jr. known as [[Stan Jolley]] (1926-2012), and the late Sandra Jolley Carson (1919–1986), the former wife of actor [[Forrest Tucker]] and the widow of actor [[Jack Carson]]. Sandra Jolley was originally an [[Earl Carroll]] showgirl. Jolley was hence the father-in-law of Tucker from 1940–1950 and of Carson from 1961 until Carson's death in 1963.<ref name=isjolley/>

Jolley died of [[emphysema]] at the age of seventy-eight at the [[Motion Picture Country Hospital]]<ref name=amctv/> in [[Woodland Hills, California|Woodland Hills]], [[California]].<ref name=imdb/> His wife died in the same facility in 2003.<ref name=isjolley/> The Jolleys are interred at [[Forest Lawn Memorial Park (Hollywood Hills)|Forest Lawn Memorial Park]] in the [[Hollywood Hills]] neighborhood of [[Los Angeles, California|Los Angeles]].<ref name=cemetery>{{cite web|url=http://www.findagrave.com/cgi-bin/fg.cgi?page=gr&GRid=5895398|title=I. Stanford Jolley|publisher=findagrave.com|accessdate=February 6, 2010}}</ref>

Pamela "Brooke" Tucker offered this reflection of her grandfather: "The most important thing about my grandfather was that he was the antithesis of all the villains he portrayed. He was a gentleman and a gentle man. He was ALWAYS interested in what the other person had to say and when you met him, he made you feel as though you were very important and special. All of my friends growing up loved him.<ref name=isjolley/>

Jolley's grave marker reads:

I. Stanford Jolley<br>
Loving Husband And Father<br>
1900-1978<br>
A Gentle Man And As Jolly By Nature As He Was By Name<br>
Loved By All and Especially His Family<ref name=cemetery/>
